Your first leather wallet

Four hand tools. One piece of vegetable-tanned hide. One afternoon. You'll come away with a slim bifold wallet that will last twenty years — and the foundation of a skill you can build on for the rest of your life.

Skill level Complete beginner
Time needed 3–4 hours
Starter budget £35–50
Step 01

What you need to get started

The leatherwork industry is full of expensive tools marketed at beginners who don't know better yet. Here's the honest list — what actually matters, and why.

The four tools that do everything

Swivel knife or sharp craft knife

For cutting your leather to shape. A Stanley knife with a fresh blade is fine to start. A swivel knife comes later if you want to carve patterns.

Budget: £5–8

Pricking iron (4mm spacing) + mallet

Makes the stitch holes. A wooden or rubber mallet works. Don't use a metal hammer — it'll damage the iron over time.

Budget: £12–18 for both

Two blunt harness needles

Saddle-stitch uses two needles simultaneously — one on each end of a single thread. Blunt tips prevent piercing the thread accidentally.

Budget: £3–5 for a pack

Metal ruler + cutting mat

You likely have these already. If not, a 30cm steel ruler and an A3 cutting mat. Never cut against a plastic ruler.

Budget: £10–15 if buying new

Skip these for now

Edge bevellers, stitching chisels, wing dividers, bone folders, burnishing wheels — you'll see these in starter kits. They're real tools you'll eventually want. But not yet. Get one project done first.

The leather

Buy vegetable-tanned leather, 2–2.5mm thick. This is the traditional stuff — tanned with plant matter rather than chromium salts. It's stiffer, it ages beautifully, and it takes a burnished edge without any fuss.

For a first wallet you need a piece roughly 30cm × 20cm. That's about a quarter of a "belly" cut — the cheapest part of the hide.

Where to buy (UK)

  • Abbey England — best quality veg-tan in the UK, reliable grading
  • Le Prevo Leathers — good starter packs, fast delivery
  • Rocky Mountain Leather Supply — if you're in North America

Avoid Amazon for leather. The listings are inconsistent and you have no way to check the grade.

Thread

Waxed linen thread, 0.8mm. Tiger Thread is the industry standard and costs £6 a spool — worth every penny over the cheap stuff, which frays and breaks. A natural or mid-tan colour looks best on your first wallet.

Step 02

The essential techniques

Three things to learn before you start the project. Spend 20 minutes on each.

1. Cutting clean lines

Leather is unforgiving — a wobbly cut is permanent. The key is a fresh blade, a metal ruler, and two or three light passes rather than one heavy cut. Let the knife do the work. Don't force it.

Practise on a scrap piece first: cut a 10cm × 5cm rectangle with perfectly square corners. Don't move on until the edges are clean and the corners are 90°.

2. Punching stitch holes

Mark your stitch line 3mm from the edge using a wing divider or by eye. Hold the pricking iron vertical, not angled — angled holes create slanted stitches. One firm strike with the mallet. Let the iron's weight do the work.

The holes should go cleanly through in one hit. If you're hacking at it, your leather is probably too thick or your iron needs cleaning.

3. The saddle stitch

This is the stitch that holds everything. Unlike machine stitching, a broken saddle-stitch thread doesn't unravel — each stitch locks the previous one. It's why a well-made leather wallet lasts decades.

Thread a needle on each end of a length of thread (arm-span × 2). Pass the left needle through the first hole, then cross the right needle through the same hole, trapping the left thread. Pull both sides equally taut. Repeat. Keep tension consistent.

How long does this take to learn?

Your first 30cm of stitching will look rough. Your second length will be better. By the end of your first wallet, it'll be good. That's the normal curve — don't skip practising on scrap first.

Step 03

The project: a slim bifold wallet

Simple, useful, and genuinely impressive to make by hand. This design holds four to six cards and folded notes — slim enough for a front pocket.

Cut list

Outer shell 19cm × 9.5cm × 1
Card pockets 9cm × 7.5cm × 2

Assembly order

  1. Cut all three pieces. Check corners are square.
  2. Skive (thin) the edges of the card pockets slightly so they don't bulk up when folded.
  3. Mark and punch stitch holes along the bottom and sides of each card pocket.
  4. Position each pocket on the outer shell (one per side). Use a bulldog clip or binder clips to hold while you stitch — no glue needed yet.
  5. Saddle-stitch each pocket in place. Lock your stitches at start and end by backstitching two holes.
  6. Trim any uneven edges with a fresh blade against a ruler.
  7. Burnish the edges: wet the edge lightly, rub with a piece of smooth hardwood (or the back of a spoon) in quick strokes until the fibres compact and shine.
  8. Fold the wallet in half. Apply gentle pressure. Leave flat under a heavy book overnight.

The most common beginner mistake

Rushing the edge finishing. Unburnished edges look cheap and feel rough. Spend the extra ten minutes — it's the thing people notice first.

Step 04

Your pathway to mastery

You've made a wallet. Here's where leatherwork can take you — at whatever pace suits you.

✓

Beginner — you're here

Straight cuts, saddle stitch, simple flat construction. You can make a wallet, a keychain, a simple bookmark.

2

Competent — 3–6 months

Gussets, box stitching, edge dyeing, rivets and snaps. Projects: a pouch, a belt, a glasses case.

3

Skilled — 1–2 years

Pattern drafting, bag construction, lining, hardware fitting. Projects: a messenger bag, a tool roll, a structured handbag.

4

Expert — 3+ years

Wet moulding, carving, dyeing, saddlery-grade work. Projects: custom holsters, briefcases, bespoke commissions.
