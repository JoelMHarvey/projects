# A Fresh Start

---

## CHAPTER ONE

The Chuo line at eight-fifteen was its own kind of faith.

Daniel had stopped marvelling at it somewhere around year three: the absolute compression of bodies, the way two hundred people could occupy a space designed for eighty and somehow produce less friction than a British queue of four. No elbows. No sighs directed at anyone. A man in a suit read a paperback held at chest height, turning pages with the only finger he had room to move. A schoolgirl slept standing up, her head finding the shoulder of a stranger who bore this without acknowledgement, which was its own form of kindness.

He had tried to explain this to his mother on a video call once. *It's not that they don't mind*, he'd said. *It's that they've agreed, without talking about it, that this is just what morning is.* His mother had said that sounded exhausting. Daniel had said it sounded like civilization, and they'd changed the subject.

Shinjuku. Yotsuya. Ochanomizu. He knew the stations now the way he knew his own breathing, not by reading the signs but by the particular pitch of the announcement, the exact drag of deceleration, the view of the Kanda River appearing briefly between buildings like something remembered.

He got off at Tokyo Station and let the current carry him through the concourse.

The omiyage wall near the Yaesu exit had been refreshed since Friday, he noticed, the way he noticed most things about Japan, with a small involuntary pleasure. Hiroshima momiji manju in their maple-leaf tins. Hokkaido white chocolate. Zunda mochi in neat rows behind glass. Someone in his office had been to Kyoto over the weekend; there would be yatsuhashi on the communal table by nine, the box passed around with two hands, everyone taking one piece, nobody taking two.

He bought a canned coffee from a vending machine (*BOSS*, the brand, with its odd portrait of a pipe-smoking Western man) and walked to the office thinking about the painting class.

---

He was, objectively, the worst student Tanaka-sensei had ever taught.

This was not false modesty. Tanaka-sensei had told him so directly in the third session, then laughed for a long time, then corrected his brush grip again. The Tuesday evening class ran from six to eight in a community centre in Kojimachi: twelve women, average age sixty-seven, and Daniel. They painted still-lifes in oils. They were uniformly excellent. Daniel's persimmons looked like something that had fallen from a height.

What he was good at was the surrounding activity. The unfolding of the plastic drop cloths. The particular negotiations over the best light. Yamamoto-san, who was eighty-one and had opinions about cadmium yellow that she shared with the intensity of someone who had been wronged by it personally. Ogawa-san, who brought homemade onigiri in a bento box and distributed them at the break with the air of someone performing a small, necessary sacrament.

He'd joined because Kenji had suggested he needed something that was *his*, not theirs, not Tokyo-the-place-they-lived-in-together but something he'd chosen without knowing the outcome. Kenji was thoughtful like that. It occasionally made Daniel feel observed, which he supposed was accurate.

*How are the old ladies*, Kenji would ask, and Daniel would report, and Kenji would listen with what Daniel recognised as genuine interest, because Kenji was genuinely interested in people in a way that still, after eight years, Daniel found quietly extraordinary.

Tonight Yamamoto-san's persimmons had glowed like small lanterns. Daniel's had glowed like small catastrophes. Tanaka-sensei had patted his arm.

*Better*, she said. *The left one.*

He looked at the left one for a long time, trying to see what she saw.

---

The bike ride home was the best part of the day, which he knew even as it was happening, which was a kind of luck he'd learned to recognise.

August in Tokyo had a specific smell: warm concrete releasing the day's heat, yakitori smoke from somewhere, the faint sweetness of convenience store air drifting through an automatic door. He and Kenji had bought secondhand bikes three summers ago and the ride home from the station had become, without discussion, a ritual: Kenji waited at the east exit, they stopped at the konbini for whatever they needed, they took the longer route along the river if neither of them was tired.

Tonight neither of them was tired.

*Tanaka-sensei said my persimmons were better*, Daniel said.

*The left one*, Kenji said.

*How did you—*

*She always says the left one.*

They rode in comfortable quiet for a while. A cat sat on a wall and watched them pass with total indifference. Someone's window was open and a game show made brief noise and was gone. The river was dark and flat and the lights of the opposite bank lay on it in wavering columns.

---

The apartment was on the fourth floor, small in the way Tokyo apartments were small, not cramped but *considered*, every surface doing two jobs, the books shelved to the ceiling, Kenji's architectural drawings rolled and stacked in a corner that had been specifically designed, Daniel understood now, to receive exactly that. The balcony fit two chairs and a row of plants Kenji tended with a consistency that Daniel associated with character.

Kenji was watering them now, the sliding door open to the warm night, the television on behind him.

Daniel opened the refrigerator. *We have rice*, he said. *And that tofu from yesterday.*

*There's miso.*

*There's miso*, Daniel agreed.

The news was on. He was aware of it in the way he was usually aware of it, as a texture, a presence, the cadence of the announcer's voice rising and falling over things that were happening somewhere that wasn't here. He caught *territorial waters* in the English chyron at the bottom of the screen. He caught something about North Korea, an economic zone, a statement from Beijing that had been characterised as *firm*.

Kenji came in from the balcony and slid the door closed.

*Tofu is fine*, he said.

*Tofu is fine*, Daniel said.

He started the rice. Kenji changed the channel to something with a chef and a lot of vegetables, and they stood together in the kitchen while the water came to the boil, and the city went on existing around them, enormous and various and entirely itself, in the way it always had, in the way that felt, if you were not paying careful attention, as though it always would.

## CHAPTER TWO

The apartment in Putuo was four hundred and twelve square feet, which Mei knew precisely because Wei had told her when they signed the lease and she had been doing the calculation ever since.

Not unhappily. She wanted to be clear about that, at least to herself. The apartment was theirs, which still felt improbable at twenty-eight, and it had a window in the kitchen that caught the morning light in a way that made even reheated congee feel considered. She had put a small succulent on the sill. It had not died. This felt, in the context of Shanghai, like an achievement.

The city was thirty minutes by metro, which meant she was in her classroom at Hongqiao Primary by seven forty-five, which meant she was writing the day's vocabulary on the board (*library*, *curious*, *discover*) before most of her students had finished breakfast. She liked this part. The empty classroom with its thirty small chairs and its laminated alphabet frieze and the smell of chalk and floor cleaner, the brief window of order before the day became itself.

She taught English to children who were eight years old. Some of them were already better than their parents. A boy named Dingding could produce a British accent with an accuracy that made visiting teachers laugh. He'd learned from YouTube videos, he said, which he watched in secret because his mother thought it was too much screen time. Mei had told him to keep watching.

*Language is a door*, she told them, which she had heard from her own teacher at fourteen and which she had believed completely ever since. *The more languages you have, the more doors you can open.*

She believed this. She wrote it on the board some mornings when she arrived early and the city was still finding itself outside the window.

---

Wei worked in infrastructure logistics for a municipal contractor, which meant he spent a great deal of time on spreadsheets and telephone calls that he summarized for her in the evenings with the patient specificity of someone who did not notice that her eyes had gone slightly soft.

He had grown up in Chengdu, the youngest of three, in a household where his father had worked for the same state construction firm for thirty-one years and retired with a certificate and a photograph and a certainty that this was what a life well-lived looked like. Wei had taken a different path (Shanghai, the contractor, the logistics work that required precision rather than loyalty to a single institution) and had never been entirely sure his father approved, though his father had never said so directly, which was its own form of saying so. He had spent ten years proving something to a man who didn't know proof was being offered.

The pregnancy had changed this. His father had called when Mei told him the news and spoken at length about responsibility and continuity and the importance of providing stability, and Wei had listened and felt, for the first time, that they were speaking the same language, not the language of ambition or approval but something older, the language of a man who understands that the point of building things is what lives inside them afterward.

He wanted to give his child what his father had given him: the certainty of ground. A place that was theirs. Walls that held.

When the letter came he had read it twice and thought: *this is what I have been building toward.* Not the specific letter (he hadn't known it was coming) but the shape of it. The offer of something solid in exchange for the willingness to begin.

She loved him for his specificity. The specificity meant he cared. He cared about everything at approximately the same sustained temperature: his work, their savings account, the creak in the bathroom cabinet he had been meaning to fix since March, the question of whether they should get a bigger rice cooker. She had married him at twenty-five and felt, three years in, that she understood the shape of him fully. This was not boredom. This was something better than boredom, a kind of relief, like knowing where the light switch is in the dark.

The pregnancy had happened the way they had planned it, which was also characteristic of Wei.

She was eleven weeks. Her body was doing things she had read about but not prepared for. On the metro she stood near the doors and breathed carefully. Shanghai at rush hour had its own physics: the compression, the collective negotiation of space, the way everyone arrived at their stop having survived something that had no name. She was becoming aware, for the first time, that this was not a city designed around what her body was currently doing. The distances. The noise. The apartment's four hundred and twelve square feet.

She had not said this to Wei. She was still deciding how to say it.

---

The letter came on a Thursday.

Wei opened it at the kitchen table while she made tea, and she knew from the way he went quiet (Wei's silences had different qualities; this one was the quality of reading something twice) that it was not a bill.

*Come and look at this*, he said.

It was printed on good paper. The header was official, the red and gold she associated with things that were decided, things that were real. She read it standing up, which meant she finished before she was ready to finish.

*Pioneer Family Resettlement Programme. New Eastern Development Zone.* Priority consideration for young professional families. Enhanced housing allocation. Salary supplement. Educational infrastructure in active development. A chance, the letter said, to be part of something historic. To build, not just inhabit.

There was a photograph of mountains. Green and very steep and covered in trees she didn't recognise.

*Japan*, she thought, before the thought completed itself into something she could examine. She put the thought down.

*It's a big move*, she said.

*It's a big opportunity*, Wei said, which was not disagreement exactly, but was not the same thing.

She looked at the photograph again. Clean air. Space. A house, a real house, the letter implied, not an apartment, not four hundred and twelve square feet. A garden, perhaps. Room for a child to move through without negotiating.

She thought about her classroom. Thirty small chairs. *Language is a door.*

She thought about Dingding and his secret YouTube videos and his British accent.

*What do you think?* Wei said, and the way he said it told her he had already thought.

She looked at the photograph of the mountains for a moment longer.

*I think*, she said, *that it's a big opportunity.*

Wei smiled. He reached across and put his hand over hers, and outside the kitchen window Shanghai went on being Shanghai, enormous and various and entirely itself, and she felt, underneath the thing she hadn't said, a loosening. As though something had been decided that she hadn't known was weighing on her.

A fresh start.

The phrase arrived without her choosing it. She tested its weight.

It seemed, that evening, to hold.

## CHAPTER THREE

The thing about working for an American bank was that the Americans talked.

Not carelessly. They were careful men and women, expensively educated, accustomed to discretion in the specific way that people are accustomed to discretion when discretion is professionally required. But they talked in the way that people talk when they believe they are among themselves, which Daniel had learned, over six years at Hargrove Pacific, was a belief he could cultivate with very little effort. He was British, which helped. He was quiet, which helped more. He had a face that people read as uninvested.

He was, in fact, always listening.

The conversations had changed in texture over the past four months. Not the content. The content remained professionally circumspect, the language of markets and exposure and repositioning. But the texture underneath. The slight acceleration. The way certain phrases arrived and then were redirected: *given the regional outlook*, *contingency positioning*, *if the situation develops*. The way Marcus, who ran APAC risk and had never in six years mentioned his family, had mentioned in March that his wife and daughters were visiting her parents in Connecticut and then had not mentioned them returning.

Daniel had started a note on his phone in February. He titled it *camping ideas* in case Kenji ever looked, which Kenji never did because Kenji trusted him completely, which was something he tried not to think about too directly.

---

The Tuesday painting class had been cancelled.

He'd found out by arriving. The community centre door was locked, which was unusual, and then a laminated sign was taped to it, which said something in Japanese he mostly understood (*temporary closure, facility maintenance, apologies for the inconvenience*) and he stood on the pavement outside for a moment before checking his phone.

Tanaka-sensei had not messaged. She had a group chat (twelve women and Daniel, his name transliterated in katakana in a way he found privately delightful) and there was nothing in it since last week's session.

He called her number.

The tone rang seven times and stopped. Not voicemail. Just stopped.

He stood outside the locked community centre until a konbini delivery bicycle went by and the normalcy of it felt like a rebuke. Then he walked to the station.

---

That evening he ordered a water purification system. Compact, trail-rated, sent to the office address and logged as equipment samples, a fiction nobody had questioned because nobody had looked.

The backpack was in the wardrobe behind his winter coats. He was aware of its presence the way you are aware of something you have not told the truth about. It contained: the tent, the sleeping mat, two sets of thermal underlayers, the gas canisters and the compact stove, iodine tablets as backup to the purifier, a first aid kit, high-calorie rations for two weeks, a hand-cranked radio, a paper map of the Yatsugatake range (paper, because paper doesn't need charging) and a photograph of the cottage in Nagano that Kenji's parents had owned since before Kenji was born. The photograph was not for navigation. He knew why it was there. He did not examine this.

He added the water purifier. He repacked the bag. He returned it to the wardrobe behind his winter coats.

---

*What did you do today*, Kenji said. He was at the kitchen counter cutting cucumber, the precise unhurried cuts of someone who approaches this as a thing worth doing well.

*Class was cancelled*, Daniel said. *I walked around. Went to the office for a bit.*

*Everything okay?*

*Tanaka-sensei wasn't answering her phone.*

*She's probably travelling*, Kenji said. *Her sister is in Osaka.*

*Right*, Daniel said. *Probably that.*

He opened the refrigerator. He looked at its contents for a moment without seeing them.

*Marcus moved his family back to the States*, he said, with the careful casualness he'd been practising since February.

*The American?* Kenji said. *He's been doing that APAC analysis.*

*Right.*

*Family reasons?*

*He didn't say.*

Kenji made a small sound that meant he'd filed this and moved on. He slid the cucumber into a bowl. The news was on in the other room: territorial waters again, a Chinese naval exercise described as *routine*, a parliamentary question in Tokyo that had been characterised as *strongly worded*.

*You've been buying outdoor things*, Kenji said.

Daniel looked at him.

*I've seen the packages*, Kenji said. He was not looking up from the bowl. *You bought a tent last month. Before that the gas canisters.*

A silence.

*I've been thinking about hiking*, Daniel said. *Properly. The Alps, maybe. We haven't done it in years.*

Kenji was quiet for a moment. Then he said: *We should go before it gets cold.*

He said it with simple warmth. He meant it. He was already planning the route somewhere in the part of his mind that planned things, already thinking about which weekends, already looking forward to it: the two of them on a mountain path in the autumn light, the particular pleasure of being somewhere that required effort to reach.

Daniel said *yes, definitely* and got out the rice cooker and did not say anything else.

Later, when Kenji was asleep, he lay in the dark and listened to the city. The distant logic of the expressway. A train somewhere. Tokyo being Tokyo, inexhaustible, indifferent, itself.

He thought about Tanaka-sensei's phone ringing seven times and stopping.

He thought about the photograph of the cottage behind his winter coats.

He did not sleep for a long time.

## CHAPTER FOUR

Golden Week began on the twenty-ninth of April.

Daniel booked the tickets on a Tuesday in March, which gave them five weeks, which felt both like too long and not long enough in a way he was learning to live with. He booked seats on the Azusa limited express from Shinjuku to Chino, where they would rent a car. He booked it the way he did everything now, with a surface layer of ordinariness covering something he could not name to anyone. He told himself he was wrong. He told himself this daily, with diminishing conviction.

He had been in a meeting on the fourteenth of February, a date he remembered because there had been small chocolate hearts on the conference table, a concession to the Westernness of the client, when Marcus had taken a call outside the glass partition and stood in the corridor with his back to the room, which was something Marcus never did. When he returned he had picked up his chocolate heart and eaten it without looking at it and said *let's revisit the Asia-Pacific exposure conversation*.

That was all. That was the whole of it.

But Daniel had worked with Marcus for four years and understood that *revisit* meant *reduce* and *Asia-Pacific* meant *now* and the particular quality of his silence for the rest of the meeting meant something that Daniel had filed without examining, the way you file a sound in the night, and then examined later in the dark.

He had looked at the risk reports after that. Nobody had told him not to. They were internally accessible, the kind of document that circulated freely among people with his clearance level because they were technical and dense and most people read the summary. Daniel read the full reports. He had always read the full reports, which was either diligence or a personality flaw depending on who you asked.

The language in February had been *elevated regional instability*. In March it had become *acute transition risk*. There were models. The models had timelines, expressed as probability distributions, the kind of language that meant nothing to people who didn't know how to read it and meant everything to people who did. The peak probability window was the final week of April through the first week of May.

Golden Week. When Tokyo empties. When half the country is in transit. When the disruption of normal patterns is already factored in.

He had closed the report. He had sat for a moment in the blue light of his monitor. Then he had opened a new tab and looked up Kenji's parents' address in Nagano prefecture, which he already knew, and then he had looked at photographs of the mountain road that led there from Chino, and then he had sat for another moment, and then he had closed the browser and gone to make coffee.

That had been in March.

---

He raised it on a Sunday, which was when Kenji was most himself: unhurried, in the apartment clothes he'd had since university, making something slow for lunch.

*I've been thinking about your family's place*, Daniel said. *In Nagano.*

Kenji looked up. The expression was one Daniel recognised: mild surprise, genuine attention. Kenji never gave partial attention.

*Obāchan's cottage*, Kenji said.

*You said it's been empty since she died.*

*Three years now. My parents keep meaning to deal with it. Nobody deals with it.*

*What would dealing with it mean?*

Kenji turned back to the stove. *Clearing it out. Fixing things. The roof was already going when she was alive. She never let anyone touch it.*

*I'd like to see it*, Daniel said. *I thought we could go for Golden Week. I could help clear it up. We could make a start on it at least — it'd be a shame to let it just—*

*You want to renovate my grandmother's cottage in Nagano.*

*As a summer retreat*, Daniel said. *From the heat. We always say we'll get out of Tokyo in August and we never do.*

Kenji was quiet for a moment. The thing he did when he was checking something internally, running the idea through some part of himself that processed slowly and accurately.

*It's in quite bad shape*, he said.

*I like a project.*

*You painted a persimmon that looked like a collapsed building.*

*Structural work*, Daniel said. *Not aesthetic.*

Kenji made a sound that was not quite a laugh but was adjacent. He was quiet again. Outside the kitchen window a pigeon landed on the railing of the next building and looked at something with total conviction.

*My parents will think we've lost our minds*, Kenji said.

*Tell them it's my idea.*

*It is your idea.*

*Then it'll be easy to explain.*

Kenji stirred something. The smell of dashi moved through the apartment. He said, without turning: *Okay. Golden Week.*

Daniel said *great* and looked at his coffee and waited for his pulse to settle.

---

He booked the tickets that afternoon, on his phone, while Kenji read in the other room. He also, quietly, checked the camping supply website he'd been monitoring and ordered three additional weeks of rations, which brought the backpack's total to five weeks for two people at reduced consumption.

He had also, three weeks earlier, transferred a significant sum from his Hargrove salary account to a personal account in his mother's name in the UK. He had not told his mother. He intended to tell her it was for safekeeping. He had not told Kenji.

He put his phone down on the coffee table and looked at the ceiling.

In the other room Kenji turned a page. The refrigerator hummed. From somewhere below, the sound of a child on a bicycle, calling something in Japanese to someone Daniel couldn't see. Tokyo being generous with itself, being enormous and particular and alive, being all the things it reliably was.

He had told himself he was wrong daily for six weeks.

He was not wrong.

He looked at the booking on his phone again. Two seats, reserved, April twenty-seventh. Two days before Golden Week began. Two days, he'd calculated, would be enough.

He put the phone face-down on the table.

*What do you want to do tonight?* Kenji called from the other room.

*Whatever you want*, Daniel said.

*There's a film. That French one we missed.*

*Perfect*, Daniel said. *That sounds perfect.*

He did not move from the couch. He sat very still for a moment, the kind of still that has effort in it, and then he got up and went to the other room where Kenji was waiting, because that was what you did. You went to the other room. You watched the film. You let the evening be what it was for as long as it could be, because the alternative was to sit alone with what you knew, and knowing alone was its own kind of devastation.

## CHAPTER FIVE

The house was bigger than the photographs.

Mei stood in the genkan (the small recessed entrance, a design feature she didn't have a word for yet, a little step down that you apparently weren't supposed to step over with your shoes on, which she knew because Wei had read it in the relocation handbook and told her three times on the plane) and looked at the rooms opening away from her and felt something she couldn't immediately name.

Not disappointment. The opposite. It was just that the photographs had been taken in a certain flat official light that had made everything seem provisional, and standing in it now she could see the rooms had depth, had age, had the particular gravity of a place where things had actually happened. The tatami in the main room. The sliding screens with their wooden frames gone silver at the edges. A window that looked out on the mountain and framed it, she realised, exactly, as though someone had considered the view.

Someone had. Someone had built this house around that view.

She didn't know why that thought arrived with an edge on it.

*It's perfect*, she said.

Wei was already checking the water pressure.

---

The relocation cohort for their sector was forty families. Mei knew this from the briefings in Shanghai, the careful presentations with their graphs of development projections and infrastructure timelines and photographs of mountains that had turned out to be accurate photographs, which she'd half-expected them not to be. The families were mostly young. Mostly couples. Quite a few pregnancies, which she'd noticed in the way she noticed everything related to pregnancy now, with a kind of sonar, an awareness that seemed to have arrived with the second line on the test and had not left.

There was a community centre. There was a school under construction, which would be open by September. The school would be excellent, the briefings said. New facilities. New curriculum.

She had asked what curriculum and the presenter had said *national standard* and moved to the next slide.

The shops in the nearest town were new. The signs were new. A supermarket with a bright red fascia she recognised from Putuo, the same chain, the same logo, stocked with the same brands she'd bought every week for three years. She had found this comforting on the drive up and was now, standing in the kitchen of the house, finding it slightly less comforting without knowing why. Like wearing your own coat in someone else's house.

The buildings the shops occupied were not new. The buildings were old in a way the signs weren't, in a way that made the signs look like name tags pressed onto a stranger's chest. The wood was dark and particular. The proportions were unlike anything she knew from Shanghai. She had stood outside the supermarket while Wei got cash from the ATM and looked at the wooden beam above the entrance, the original beam, untouched, running the full width of the shopfront, and tried to read the faint impression in it where something had been mounted and removed.

She couldn't make it out. The beam had been sanded.

---

There were other families on the road. She'd waved at a woman hanging washing two houses down who had waved back with the slightly effortful warmth of someone who was also trying. A family from Chengdu, she'd heard from the relocation coordinator. Most of the cohort were from somewhere else: Shanghai, Wuhan, Chengdu, one family from Harbin who'd seemed as bewildered by the heat as by everything else. Nobody was from here. Nobody knew how things worked.

This should have felt like solidarity. It mostly did. There was just, underneath it, a texture she kept noticing, a sense that *how things work* was not a neutral question, that it pointed somewhere she wasn't being directed to look.

How did things work before we arrived?

She didn't say this. She noted it the way she noted the carved wooden marker at the gate of the house (characters she could partially read, a name and possibly a number, the style of an older hand) and set it aside with the intention of returning to it, the way she set aside things in the classroom that needed more time than the moment allowed.

---

The shrine was at the bend in the road, twenty metres past their gate.

She almost missed it. It was small: a wooden structure no taller than her knee, a little roof, a cup in front of it. She noticed it on her second walk along the road, the afternoon of the first day, when she'd gone out for air and for the particular necessity of moving her body that the pregnancy had introduced without asking.

The cup held flowers. Small white ones, mountain flowers she couldn't name, the kind that don't come from a shop. They were fresh. The petals had not browned at the edges.

Someone had put them there. Recently, today or yesterday or the day before. Someone who knew this bend in the road, who had a reason to stop here, who had something to commemorate or ask for at this particular spot.

She looked at the shrine for a moment. Then she looked at the road in both directions, which was empty, which was just road and trees and the mountain above.

She walked back to the house.

The air was very clean. The mountain sat where it had always sat, solid and enormous and entirely without opinion. In the house she could see Wei moving past the kitchen window, checking something, making a note, his face wearing the expression it wore when he was doing something useful.

She put her hand on her stomach, which she'd been doing constantly since April, a reflex she couldn't stop.

*Who was here before us*, she thought. And then: *where did they go.*

She went inside. She did not say it aloud. There was dinner to make, and boxes still to unpack, and the house was bigger than the photographs and had a window that framed the mountain exactly, and these were good things. These were reasons she had come.

She went inside and did not look back at the road.

## CHAPTER SIX

The Azusa left Shinjuku at nine forty-seven and Daniel watched Tokyo unspool through the window with the particular attention of someone memorising a face.

He had not told himself he was doing this. He was doing it.

The city gave itself up slowly: first the density softening, the towers thinning, then the low suburban spread with its pachinko signs and its car dealerships and its community centres with their noticeboards he'd never been able to read at speed, and then the rice paddies beginning, and then the mountains appearing in the distance like something that had been waiting patiently for the buildings to get out of the way.

Kenji was asleep within twenty minutes. This was characteristic. He could sleep on any train, in any seat, with the immediate totality of someone who had made a decision. His head was tipped slightly toward the window. His hands were loose in his lap. He had a notebook on the fold-down tray because he'd been making sketches of the cottage layout since breakfast, measuring from memory, noting what would need to be done in the order it would need doing. The notebook was open to a page of small precise drawings. Daniel looked at them for a moment.

Kenji had drawn a roof plan. Had marked the section his grandmother had refused to let anyone fix. Had noted, in his small architectural hand, *check rafter condition, likely rot, source local timber if possible.*

He had written *source local timber* as though local were still a thing the place would have.

Daniel looked out the window.

---

His phone had seventeen news apps. He had acquired them incrementally over the past two months, each one a different angle on the same thing he was trying to see clearly: Japanese wire services, Reuters, a Taiwanese outlet a colleague in risk had mentioned without elaborating, a Korean broadcaster, the BBC, which he had found himself trusting less the longer this went on, not from cynicism but from the specific experience of knowing more than it reported.

This morning all seventeen had been active. Territorial tensions. Naval positioning. A statement from the Foreign Ministry in Tokyo that had been described as *measured*. A Chinese spokesperson who had described the situation as *stable and under management*, which was a phrase Daniel had learned to read the way you learn to read weather.

Somewhere past Tachikawa he opened the apps again.

The Taiwanese outlet had not updated since six forty-three a.m.

He checked the timestamp twice.

He opened the Japanese wire service. Eleven forty-one a.m., a story about Golden Week travel volumes, record numbers expected, the usual. Below it, a story from the morning about the naval exercises, which had been described as *concluding*. Nothing since.

He opened Reuters. A story about European energy markets. A story about a film festival. A story timestamped forty minutes ago about the East China Sea situation being *de-escalating following diplomatic engagement*.

He looked at that phrase for a long time.

*De-escalating following diplomatic engagement* was not a phrase that arrived from nowhere. It was a phrase that was placed. Someone had decided that phrase should exist in the world at eleven fifty-eight a.m. on April the twenty-seventh, and Reuters had agreed to carry it, and now it was in seventeen million feeds and it meant, to most people, that things were getting better, and it meant to Daniel something he could feel in his sternum like a dropped weight.

He opened the BBC.

*East China Sea tensions ease as talks resume. Markets respond positively.*

He put his phone face-down on his thigh.

Outside the window, Yamanashi prefecture was being unreasonably beautiful: the mountains closer now, the sky the particular blue that seems to exist only above altitude, a river appearing and disappearing alongside the tracks. A child in the seats ahead had her face pressed to the glass. Her breath made a small cloud and she drew a circle in it with one finger and then watched it disappear.

Kenji stirred. Didn't wake. Made a small sound and resettled.

Daniel thought about the Taiwanese outlet at six forty-three a.m. He thought about what it would take to stop a Taiwanese outlet from updating. He thought about the specific sequencing of silence, not a story stopping because there was nothing to report, but a story stopping because the mechanism of reporting had encountered something that prevented it.

He thought: *two days*.

He had thought he had two days.

He picked his phone up and checked the train time. Chino at twelve fifty-one. Fifty-three minutes.

He looked at Kenji's notebook. The roof plan. *Source local timber.*

He thought: we are on the right train. We are doing the right thing. We are fifty-three minutes away.

He told himself this with the force of someone who needed it to be a complete sentence, with nothing after it, no clause beginning with *but* or *if* or *unless the timeline—*

Kenji opened his eyes.

*Where are we?* he said, with the mild unconcerned interest of someone returning from sleep without anxiety about what they might have missed.

*Nearly there*, Daniel said.

Kenji looked out the window. His face did what it sometimes did when he encountered landscape, a kind of opening, a drop in the habitual tension of being a person in a city. He looked at the mountains the way he'd looked at them since childhood, Daniel understood, with familiarity that had never curdled into the ordinary.

*My grandmother called them the old ones*, Kenji said. *She said they were here before everything and would be here after.*

Daniel said nothing.

*She wasn't religious exactly*, Kenji said. *She just thought some things deserved respect for how long they'd managed to exist.*

The train curved and the mountains swung in the window and for a moment they filled it completely, just that, just the mountains, before the valley came back and the river and the ordinary world reassembled itself around them.

*Chino next stop*, the announcement said, in Japanese, in the voice Daniel knew, the particular cadence he had learned the way he'd learned the stations on the Chuo line.

He picked up his bag.

He did not look at his phone again.

## CHAPTER SEVEN

The key was under the same stone.

Kenji crouched and lifted it, a flat grey river stone, unremarkable, exactly where it had always been, and held the key for a moment before standing. Daniel watched him do this and said nothing. There were things Kenji did that required the space around them to stay still.

The door swelled in its frame and needed a shoulder. Inside, the smell arrived first: old tatami, wood smoke long-extinguished, something herbal and faintly sweet that Daniel couldn't name and didn't ask about. The engawa ran the length of the back of the house, a narrow wooden veranda overlooking what had been the garden, now overgrown, the grass moving in the mountain draft. The irori was in the main room, the open hearth sunk into the floor, its iron kettle hook still hanging above the ash. Three years of ash, undisturbed.

Kenji stood in the doorway of the main room and looked at it.

*She used to have the fire going by the time I woke up*, he said. *Every morning. I never once saw her light it. It was just — already burning.*

Daniel set his bag down. He looked at the room: the tatami worn pale at the threshold, the alcove with its single hanging scroll, a painted mountain, the mountain outside the window. Someone had set it there decades ago so that the painted mountain and the real one would speak to each other through the glass. He noticed this and felt something move through him that he didn't examine.

*How old were you*, Daniel said. *When you used to come here.*

*Every summer until I was sixteen. Then less.* Kenji moved to the window, touched the frame. *You know how it is. You think the place will always be there.*

He said it without bitterness. As a fact. The kind of fact that has been absorbed so completely it no longer hurts in the expected places.

---

They worked through the afternoon.

Kenji moved through the rooms with the quiet efficiency of someone who had been storing a to-do list for three years and was finally permitted to begin. He checked the roof from the outside, walking the perimeter, looking up with the focused squint of a man who understood what he was seeing. He ran the water. It came brown for thirty seconds and then clear. He opened every window and let the mountain air move through.

He showed Daniel where the oranges had been: a corner of the garden where three trees still stood, unpruned and leggy, last year's mikan dried on the branches like small brown lanterns. *She grew them from seeds*, he said. *She said nobody thought you could grow mikan this far north. She did it anyway.*

*Where did she keep the fox*, Daniel said.

Kenji looked at him. *I told you about the fox.*

*In Osaka. Two years ago. You'd had quite a lot of sake.*

*Ah.* Kenji looked toward the far end of the garden where the trees thickened. *There was a hutch she built. The fox came injured — a trap, she thought, from someone's farm. She kept it until it healed and then it stayed. She said it had decided.*

*What was it called.*

*She called it Gin. Silver.* He looked at the overgrown corner. *It outlived her by — I don't know. I don't know what happened to it.*

He said this simply, and turned back to the house, and Daniel followed him, and neither of them spoke for a while.

---

In the evening they went to the village.

It was five kilometres on the mountain road, the car rounding bends that Kenji took with the ease of remembered geography, his hand light on the wheel. *There's a tofu maker*, he said. *Or there was. Old man Harada. His tofu was absurd, it wasn't like — you'll understand when you taste it.* He said *when* with the simple confidence of someone who had no reason to say *if*.

Daniel watched the road.

The village announced itself with a cluster of rooftops below the tree line, the same dark wood and curved eaves he'd seen in photographs of Kenji's childhood summers. Old in the particular way of mountain villages, built against the weather, built to last, built by people who intended to stay.

The signs were in Mandarin.

Kenji stopped the car.

They sat for a moment. Outside, the village went about what it was doing: a woman walking with a bag, a truck idling outside what had been a shop and was still a shop, a child on a bicycle. All of it normal. All of it happening under signs that said, in characters that were almost but not quite the ones Kenji had grown up reading, that this was a supermarket, this was a community notice, this was the name of the road.

Daniel watched Kenji look at the buildings.

Not the signs. The buildings. He was doing the thing he did with structures, the thing Daniel had watched him do at construction sites and on long walks and in rooms he'd entered for the first time: reading the bones of a place, the load-bearing logic beneath the surface, what the proportions told you about the people who had built them and what they had believed about permanence. These buildings said *generations.* The spacing of the beams, the depth of the eaves designed against a specific kind of mountain snow, the foundations set for a specific kind of mountain ground by people who had studied that ground long enough to know its behaviour. Everything about them said: we are staying. We built for our children's children.

The signs above them said something that had nothing to do with any of that.

Kenji understood (Daniel could see him understanding) that you could change every surface of a place and the structure beneath would still be speaking. That the buildings were still saying *we are staying* in the language of load and beam and foundation, and would keep saying it long after every sign above them had been replaced, long after every name had been filed smooth.

He understood, Daniel thought, that this was also true of other things.

*'That's—'* Kenji started.

He stopped.

Daniel watched him read the signs. Watched him work through the Kanji that were familiar and the Simplified characters that were not quite, the places where the language overlapped and the places where it didn't, and watched his face do something that Daniel had never seen it do before, a kind of stillness that was not calm, that was the opposite of calm, that was the stillness of a person standing very still because moving would require acknowledging where they were standing.

*Daniel*, Kenji said.

*I know*, Daniel said.

*These signs—*

*I know.*

A silence.

The truck outside the shop finished its idling and pulled away. The child on the bicycle disappeared around a corner. The woman with the bag went through a door. The village continued, indifferent, reorganised, proceeding under its new instructions.

Kenji looked at the sign above what had been, Daniel now saw, a hardware shop. He could tell from the shape of the window, the width of the frontage, the ghost of old lettering sanded from the wooden beam above the door. The sign above it now was red and new and said something in Mandarin that Daniel couldn't read and that Kenji was reading with an expression Daniel would not describe to anyone.

*Harada-san's tofu shop*, Kenji said quietly. *It was next to the hardware shop. He had a sign in the shape of a tofu block. My grandmother said it was the most useful sign she'd ever seen.*

Daniel said nothing.

*Where is Harada-san*, Kenji said.

He did not say it to Daniel. He said it to the street, to the new signs, to whatever remained underneath them.

*Let's go back*, Daniel said.

Kenji didn't move for a moment. He was looking at the beam above the hardware shop. The sanded place where the lettering had been.

Then he started the car.

He did not speak on the road back. Daniel watched the mountain come and go between the trees and listened to the engine and did not fill the silence, because some silences are not gaps but structure, load-bearing, and Kenji needed this one to hold him up until they reached the cottage.

The key was under the same stone.

## CHAPTER EIGHT

Daniel woke at five and did not go back to sleep.

This had become usual. His body had quietly renegotiated its relationship with rest sometime in March, deciding that four or five hours was sufficient, that the remaining dark hours were better spent in a state of low vigilance that was not quite wakefulness but was not sleep either. He lay in his grandmother's spare room (Kenji was in her room, had gone there without discussion, as though it were still his) and listened to the mountain.

It was loud in the way that silent places are loud. Wind in the treeline. Something moving in the undergrowth, unhurried. The occasional knock of the house settling into the cold. He had grown up in a semi-detached in Bromley where the background was always human, traffic, televisions, the neighbours' particular rhythms, and the absence of that had taken him years to stop hearing as absence.

At six he got up and made tea on the camping stove he'd brought in the pack, because the cottage gas had been off for three years and he hadn't found the valve. He stood at the kitchen window with the mug and watched the light come.

The next house was forty metres away, slightly uphill, half-screened by a cedar that had grown since whatever photograph Kenji had seen of it. In the window, visible now in the morning light, not last night's darkness, the unmistakable fact of occupation: a box on the engawa not there yesterday, a car in the turning area, a curtain that had been opened from inside.

Someone had moved in.

He thought about the word *inhabited* and what it required. How long it took. Whether it was something you chose or something that happened to you.

At eight Kenji emerged with the notebook.

He did not mention the village. He sat at the low table in the main room with tea and the notebook and began drawing again, revised now, more detailed, measurements he'd taken by pacing the rooms with the focused practicality Daniel had always admired and was currently finding difficult to witness. The practicality was protection. Kenji was building something to do with his hands so that his mind would have somewhere to put itself.

Daniel understood this and left him to it.

He went outside to look at the roof.

The morning was sharp and clear, the kind of mountain morning that seems to have been recently laundered. The cedar tree threw a long shadow across the track. He could see the valley from here, and beyond it the next ridge, and somewhere far below, invisible, theoretical, the roads that connected this place to the world, the world that was currently being very quiet in the way of something holding its breath.

He heard her before he saw her.

A sound from the next property, not alarming, not a voice, just the specific acoustic of someone wrestling with something physical and losing. He stepped to the edge of the track and looked up.

She was on the engawa of the next house, trying to move a large cardboard box. It was clearly heavy. She had it half-tilted, one end on the decking, trying to slide it toward the door, and the problem was evident from forty metres: she was six months pregnant, possibly more, and the box was winning.

She stopped. Put her hands on her lower back. Looked at the box with an expression Daniel recognised, the expression of someone recalculating.

He stood on the track and watched.

He thought: *do not go over there.*

He thought about what Kenji would say. He thought about the light on last night, what it meant, who these people were, where they had come from, the rental car with the mainland plates. He thought about the systematic intelligence of not existing, of being two people in a cottage that wasn't listed anywhere official, of the drones that had swept the valley last night at eleven. He'd heard them, Kenji hadn't. He'd lain still in the dark and listened to them pass.

She tried the box again. Got it another six inches. Stopped again, one hand on the doorframe, breathing.

He was across the track before he had completed the thought.

---

She heard him on the path and turned, and for a moment they looked at each other across the garden gate with the mutual assessment of two people who had not planned this encounter and were now committed to it.

She was younger than he'd expected. Dark hair pulled back. A face that was, his brain reported somewhat uselessly, kind, the kind that isn't performed, that is simply the default arrangement of someone's features. She looked at him with neither alarm nor particular welcome, just the careful neutrality of a person in an unfamiliar place who has learned not to lead with assumption.

*Hello*, she said. In English.

The choice of it was precise. She had looked at him and made a calculation, not Japanese, not Chinese, something else, and offered the language most likely to bridge. It was the instinct of a teacher.

*Hello*, he said. *I'm — we're staying next door. I saw you struggling with the box.*

She looked at the box. She looked at him.

*It is very heavy box*, she said, with a slight emphasis on *very* that suggested the box had personally disappointed her.

*I can move it. If you'd like.*

A pause. The particular pause of an independent person deciding whether to accept help, which Daniel recognised because Kenji had that pause too.

*Thank you*, she said. And then: *I am Mei.*

*Daniel*, he said.

He went through the gate, which was not latched, and lifted the box. It was very heavy. He didn't let this show.

*Inside?* he said.

*Please. The main room.*

The engawa. The door. Inside: the smell of a house being claimed, cardboard and new ownership and underneath it the same thing he'd smelled in the cottage, that faint residue of long habitation. He set the box down where she indicated.

*Thank you*, she said again. She was looking at him with an openness he found, in his current state, almost painful. No guard. No second reading of the situation. Just a person who had accepted help and was grateful.

*Are you here alone*, he said, and then heard how it sounded and said: *I'm sorry — I meant, is your husband—*

*Wei is in town. For supplies.* She put her hand on the box, not to move it, just contact. *We arrived three days before.*

*We arrived yesterday.*

*From where?* A slight formality: *from where* rather than the native speaker's *from*, the extra word that marks the sentence as constructed rather than reflexive.

*Tokyo*, he said. *Originally. You?*

*Shanghai.* She said it with a simple pride, not boasting, just the way people say the name of a city they love. She looked around the room. *It is different here. The air.*

*Yes*, Daniel said. *The air is different.*

She looked at him for a moment. Something in her face moved, not suspicion, something more like the beginning of a question she hadn't formed yet. He had the feeling, acute and uncomfortable, of being seen clearly by someone who didn't yet know what they were seeing.

*You speak Japanese?* she said.

*A little. My partner is Japanese.*

*Ah.* A small nod. *There are not many here who are—* She stopped. Looked for the word. *Not from China. Most people here are from China.*

*Yes*, Daniel said. *I noticed.*

Another pause. The house around them, doing what houses do: settling, containing, holding its prior self underneath the new one.

*Well*, he said. *If you need anything moved.*

*You are very kind*, Mei said. She meant it. He could tell she meant it and felt this like a small splinter, painless now, that would matter later.

He went back down the path. Through the gate. Down the track to the cottage.

Kenji was in the doorway.

He had the notebook in his hand. He was looking at Daniel with an expression that was not quite anything yet, not anger, not fear, just very careful attention, the same attention he gave to a structural drawing, to a roof plan, to a thing that needed to be correctly understood before it could be addressed.

*There's a woman*, Daniel said. *Next door. She's pregnant and alone and she needed help with a box.*

Kenji said nothing.

*She's from Shanghai*, Daniel said.

The mountain was behind Kenji and above it the sky was the laundered blue and the cedar tree moved in the draft and somewhere in the valley something, a vehicle, maybe, or something that sounded like a vehicle, moved along a road they couldn't see.

*Come inside*, Kenji said.

He stood back from the door. Daniel went in. Kenji closed it, carefully, and stood for a moment with his hand still on the frame.

*Her name is Mei*, Daniel said.

Kenji looked at the closed door.

*I'll make breakfast*, he said.

## CHAPTER NINE

The drones came at eleven.

Daniel had learned their schedule the way you learn anything that threatens you, by paying attention until the pattern emerged. Eleven, and again at three. Sometimes two, flying parallel at different altitudes, their sound overlapping into a single low wash that moved across the valley and was gone. Sometimes one, slower, making a wider arc that included the ridge above the cottage.

He lay in the dark and listened to this one complete its arc.

Kenji was awake. Daniel could tell by his breathing, the slight shallowness of it, the absence of the particular rhythm that meant sleep. They had not discussed the drones. They had not discussed most of what needed discussing, because discussing it would make it the shape it actually was, and they were both, in their different ways, still managing the shape of it.

The drone passed. The sound thinned and was replaced by wind and the small movements of the house.

*Daniel*, Kenji said, in the dark.

*Yes.*

*How long have you known.*

Not a question. The cadence of it was different, the cadence of someone who has been holding a door closed and has decided to open it.

Daniel looked at the ceiling. *Since February*, he said. *Roughly.*

Silence.

The silence had a particular quality Daniel had not encountered before, not the silence of a conversation paused but the silence of a person rearranging something fundamental, laying it out to look at it, finding the edges of it. He had imagined this conversation many times. He had imagined Kenji's anger, or his fear, or the specific cold of someone who has been deceived by the person who should not deceive them. He had not imagined this silence. He did not know yet what it contained.

*The bank*, Kenji said.

*Yes.*

*Marcus. The American.*

*Yes.*

Another silence.

*Tell me*, Kenji said. *From the beginning. Tell me everything you knew and when you knew it.*

So Daniel told him. In the dark, in the cold cottage, with the mountain outside and the valley quiet below them, he told it from the beginning: the February meeting, the chocolate hearts on the table, Marcus in the corridor with his back to the room. The risk reports. The probability distributions. The peak window. The silence of the Taiwanese outlet at six forty-three in the morning. The tickets. The rations in the wardrobe behind his winter coats. The money in his mother's account.

He told it plainly, without apology in the telling because the telling itself was the accounting, the complete version he had not been able to give anyone, the thing he had been carrying alone in the specific loneliness of knowing something you cannot share with the person it matters most to.

Kenji listened without interrupting. Daniel could feel him listening, the quality of his attention in the dark, the way he had always listened, with his whole self, with the patience of someone who believed that the full story was worth waiting for.

When Daniel finished there was a silence longer than the others.

*You should have told me*, Kenji said.

*Yes.*

*In February. You should have told me in February.*

*I know.*

*We could have—* He stopped. *My parents.* His voice didn't change on the words, that was the thing, it didn't change, it remained level in the way of a person who is holding something very tightly and knows that releasing it now would not help anyone. *If we had gone in February. If I had known—*

*I know*, Daniel said. *I know. I told myself I wasn't certain. I told myself I might be wrong. I told myself—*

*You weren't wrong.*

*No.*

The word sat between them. A small word doing a very large amount of work.

*I didn't want to frighten you*, Daniel said. *I didn't want to — it sounds wrong to say it now. But I kept thinking: another week, more certainty, wait until I know for sure. And then I thought: if I tell him and I'm wrong, we've destroyed something. Our life here. I kept — I kept protecting the life we had instead of—*

*Instead of protecting us.*

*Yes.*

He said it into the dark and felt the full weight of it settle, which was correct, which was what the moment required. No softening. No qualification. He had made a calculation and the calculation had been wrong and Kenji's parents were in Osaka and the phone rang and rang and didn't answer.

Kenji was quiet for a long time.

Outside the window the mountain was a darker dark against the sky. The house settled. Something moved in the undergrowth below the garden, unhurried, small, a creature going about its night in complete indifference to the humans lying in the dark above it, carrying the specific weight of eight years and the specific weight of everything that had happened to those years.

*You got us here*, Kenji said finally.

*Not soon enough—*

*You got us here.* Not absolution. Something more complicated than absolution. The acknowledgement of a person who has done the full accounting and arrived at a position that is neither forgiveness nor its absence but something beyond both, the place you reach when you love someone past the point where blame is useful. *I'm angry*, he said. *I want you to know that. I'm going to be angry for a while.*

*I know*, Daniel said.

*But you got us here. And we are here. And I—* He stopped. His voice did something, recovered. *I need you to not do that again. Whatever you know. Whatever you think you're protecting me from. Tell me.*

*Yes*, Daniel said. *I will. I promise.*

Another silence. Different from the others. The door open now, the thing between them named, the anger present and acknowledged and set beside them like an object that would need to be dealt with, not tonight, but that could be borne now that it was visible.

Kenji's hand found Daniel's in the dark.

Daniel held it and looked at the ceiling and thought about February, the risk reports, the probability windows, the specific cowardice of protecting a life instead of a person, and understood that he would carry this alongside everything else now, the full inventory of what he had and hadn't done, and that this was correct, that this was what being known by someone actually meant.

The hand-crank radio was on the floor beside the bed. Daniel reached down without letting go of Kenji's hand and turned the volume dial until the static resolved into something that might be signal.

Fragments. A voice, male, Japanese, reading something in the clipped cadence of emergency broadcast. Then static. Then the voice again — *—urge all remaining—* and then white noise and then silence and then the carrier wave alone, a flat tone, steady, broadcasting nothing.

Kenji's hand tightened.

*Remaining*, he said.

*Yes*, Daniel said.

They lay in the dark and listened to the carrier wave, which was the sound of a system still running after the message had ended, faithful and empty, transmitting nothing to no one into the mountain night.

## CHAPTER TEN

Mei told Wei about the neighbour while he was unpacking the kitchen boxes.

She told it carefully: the box, the offer of help, the brief exchange. She watched Wei's face as she told it. He was unwrapping glasses in newspaper, setting them on the counter, and he did not stop unwrapping glasses but something in the efficiency of it changed slightly.

*Japanese*, he said.

*British. But his partner is Japanese. They're staying next door.*

*Staying.*

*In the cottage.*

Wei set a glass down. Picked up another. *The relocation coordinator said the properties in this sector were unoccupied.*

*Perhaps they arrived after the survey.*

*Perhaps.* He unwrapped the glass. Set it down. *Did he say how long they were staying.*

*We didn't discuss it.*

Wei nodded. The nod of someone filing rather than concluding. He picked up the next glass and Mei watched his hands and understood that the conversation had not ended, it had merely been set aside in the way Wei set aside things that needed more information before they could be resolved.

She thought about Daniel's face when she'd said *most people here are from China.* The particular quality of his stillness. The way he'd said *yes, I noticed*, not surprised, not commenting, just confirming. As though he'd arrived already knowing.

She thought about the shrine at the bend in the road. The fresh flowers, replaced now, she'd checked, replaced by whom she still hadn't determined.

*Wei*, she said.

*Mm.*

*Who was here before.*

He looked up. Not alarmed (Wei did not alarm easily) but attentive in the way of someone who has heard a question they were expecting and have been deciding how to answer.

*It was disputed territory*, he said. *The administration was complicated. There were residents, but the legal status was — it's been resolved now.*

*Resolved how.*

*Through the proper processes.* He said it with the firmness of a man who believes this, who needs to believe this, who has built a life on the reliability of proper processes. *Mei. We went through this. The briefings—*

*The briefings didn't say where the people went.*

*Resettlement. There were resettlement programmes. For the residents who—*

*Like ours*, she said.

He looked at her.

*Our programme is called resettlement*, she said. *We were resettled here. Where were the people who were here before us resettled to.*

The kitchen was quiet. The mountain light came through the window and lay on the new glasses on the counter, the wrapped newspaper, the half-empty box.

Wei looked at her for a moment with an expression she hadn't seen on him before, not quite fear, not quite sorrow, something between them, something that knew.

*We are building something new here*, he said. *That's what we came for. That's what we were told.*

*I know what we were told.*

*Mei.*

*I know*, she said. *I know. I'm just—* She put her hand on her stomach. *I want to understand where we are.*

Wei crossed the kitchen and put his arms around her from behind, his hands over hers on the curve of her stomach, his chin on her shoulder. She felt the solidity of him, the familiar weight.

*We are home*, he said. *That's where we are.*

She looked out the kitchen window. The cedar tree. The track. The cottage next door, its chimney cold in the morning.

She let herself lean back into him.

She did not say what she was thinking. She was thinking about a word she taught her students in Shanghai, a word she put on the board in the first week of term every year because she thought it was important, because she thought language should be precise.

*Erasure*, she had written. And underneath it: *the removal of all marks or traces.*

She had taught it as vocabulary. She had not thought about it as a thing that happened to places. To peoples. To the marks they left.

She let Wei hold her and looked at the cottage next door and did not say any of this aloud.

## CHAPTER ELEVEN

The notebook had forty-three pages by the end of the first week.

Kenji wrote in the mornings, at the low table, with the small precise hand he used for architectural drawings, as though the act required the same discipline, the same fidelity to what was actually there rather than what you wished were there. Daniel watched him and did not interrupt and sometimes made tea and set it beside him without speaking.

He wrote recipes first. His grandmother's dashi: the exact ratio of kombu to katsuobushi, the temperature at which the kombu should be removed, the note she had added in her own practice which was never written anywhere: *you will know by the smell, not the clock.* Her miso soup. The way she cut the tofu, not cubed but torn, she said torn tofu held the broth better, she had no patience for people who cubed it. Her tsukemono, the pickled vegetables she kept in a ceramic pot she'd been given at her wedding, the weight of the stone she placed on top to press them.

He wrote place names. Every road and path and landmark within five kilometres that he knew by name: the old names, the names people had used, not the administrative designations that had already been replaced on the new signs. The ridge the village children called *the sleeping giant* because of its profile against the sky. The stream that ran behind the forestry road that his grandmother called *the cold one* to distinguish it from the seasonal stream higher up. The pine grove where the matsutake grew, which had no official name because it didn't need one. Everyone who needed to know it knew it.

He wrote the names of people his grandmother had mentioned. Harada-san and his tofu. The Yamamoto family who had farmed the lower terrace for four generations. The priest at Ichijō-ji whose name he couldn't fully remember but whose face he could: a broad face, a shaved head, a laugh that arrived without warning. He wrote *priest at Ichijō-ji, name unclear, broad face, sudden laugh* and this felt both inadequate and necessary, the way all of it felt.

He wrote Bashō. He wrote Ryōkan. He wrote the first verse of Kokoro that he'd memorised at fourteen because a teacher had told him to and he'd been grateful ever since. He wrote the death poem of Yamamoto Tsunetomo, which his grandfather had recited to him once with a formality that had seemed excessive to a twelve-year-old and now seemed, simply, correct. He wrote the four seasons as the classical poets understood them, not as weather but as feeling, mono no aware, the bittersweet knowledge of passing, the pathos of things, the word that had no equivalent in English and that Daniel had been trying to understand for eight years and that Kenji could only say was something you knew by having lived through enough seasons to feel the turning of them as a kind of grief that was also a kind of love.

He wrote his parents' address in Osaka.

He wrote it in small characters at the bottom of a page and said nothing about it and Daniel did not ask.

---

He checked the apps first. Habit, or what habit had become, not information-gathering exactly, more like pressing a bruise to confirm it was still there. Seventeen apps. Twelve had stopped updating entirely. Three produced content about the Eastern Development Zones in the measured promotional language of state media: infrastructure timelines, resettlement milestones, a photograph of a family standing outside a new house in bright sunshine. Two still carried something that might be called news, timestamped, though the timestamps had become unreliable. He read them both. He learned nothing he didn't already know.

He put the phone face-down on the table.

He wasn't sure anymore whether he checked them for information or for the habit of looking: the reflex of a man who had spent six months reading between lines, who had trained himself to find the signal in the silence, and who now lived entirely inside the silence with no signal left to find.

He went outside. Split wood until his arms gave out. Came in. Made tea. Sat.

---

On the ninth day Kenji went to the temple.

He went in the morning, in daylight, which Daniel said nothing about because Kenji had already made the decision before he mentioned it and they both understood that the decision was not negotiable. He took the camera, a small film camera, his grandmother's, found in the drawer beneath the irori with two unexposed rolls. He had been saving it. This was what he was saving it for.

Daniel watched him go up the track and disappear around the cedar.

Kenji came back at noon.

He set the camera on the table. He sat. He did not speak immediately and Daniel did not prompt him.

*The cemetery is behind the temple*, Kenji said finally. *A small one. Forty, fifty stones. Family plots going back — some of the dates I could read were Meiji era. A hundred and forty years.*

Daniel waited.

*There was a machine*, Kenji said. *Working along the rows.*

He described it with the precision of an architect and the steadiness of a man who has decided that steadiness is what is required, though Daniel could see what the steadiness was costing him, could see it in the set of his jaw and the stillness of his hands on the table.

The machine was low and wide, moving on tracks, unhurried. An arm extended from its body with a rotary head: the kind used for surface finishing in construction, for smoothing concrete, for removing material with controlled uniformity. It moved to a stone, made contact, and worked the face of it in overlapping passes until the surface was blank. Then it moved to the next.

It had no awareness of what it was doing. This was, Kenji said, the thing he kept returning to.

*It wasn't cruel*, he said. *It wasn't — there was no malice in it. It was the same as a machine that resurfaces a road. It encountered a surface with marks on it and it removed the marks. That was its function. It had completed most of the rows.*

He looked at his hands.

*I photographed what was left*, he said. *The ones it hadn't reached. I used both rolls.*

Daniel thought about the rolls of film in the camera. Forty, fifty gravestones. The names on them: Meiji era, he'd said, a hundred and forty years of one family's dead, their names and dates and the small characters beside them that in Japanese grave markers denote the posthumous Buddhist name, the name given at death, the name that is also a kind of poem.

*I couldn't stop it*, Kenji said. Not with guilt, simply as a fact, as coordinates, as the precise location of where he was.

*No*, Daniel said.

*I watched it finish a stone. An old one — the date was 1887. I could read the name.* He paused. *Nakamura. Same character as mine.*

The kitchen was very quiet.

*I don't know if we're related*, Kenji said. *The name is common. But they were here. They were in this valley in 1887 and someone cut their name into stone so it would last and it lasted a hundred and forty years.* He looked at the camera on the table. *And then it lasted until this morning.*

Daniel reached across the table. Kenji looked at his hand for a moment, then turned his own over, and they sat like that while the mountain light moved across the room.

They sat like that for a long time. The mountain light moved across the room and reached the far wall and began to go.

Kenji did not move. Daniel did not move. The irori held its cold ash. The notebook held its pages. The camera sat on the table between them with its two spent rolls: forty, fifty gravestones, whatever was left before the machine completed its rows, the last record of names that would otherwise have no record, that existed now only in a film canister and in the specific memory of the man sitting across the table whose hands had not steadied since he set them down.

Daniel watched Kenji's face.

He had an architect's relationship to ruins. Daniel had understood this slowly, over years. Kenji could look at a damaged thing and see simultaneously what it had been and what remained. He did not look away from damage. He had never been able to look away from damage, which was either his greatest strength or the thing that cost him most, depending on the day.

Today it was costing him.

Daniel watched him arrive, from somewhere very far inside himself, at a decision. Not a thought, something below thought, something the body makes before the mind catches up. Kenji's hands steadied. His shoulders dropped a fraction. He looked at the table and then at the window and then he stood up.

*I'm going to make soup*, he said.

Not *I need to do something* or *I can't sit here*. Just the soup. The specific soup. His grandmother's dashi, the ratio she had never written down, the temperature you know by the smell not the clock. The act of making it was its own argument against the machine in the cemetery, not a rebuttal, not a consolation, simply the insistence of continuing, of the hands finding the next thing they knew how to do.

He went to the kitchen.

Daniel sat for another moment at the table.

Then he got up and followed.

## CHAPTER ELEVEN (b)

He brought it up that afternoon.

They were outside, Kenji splitting wood (he had taken over the splitting entirely, had established it as his task in the way he established things, quietly and without discussion) and Daniel was stacking, and the rhythm of it had produced the particular silence that physical work produces, the silence that is not empty but full of the sound of what isn't being said.

*I want to go back*, Kenji said. *To the clearing.*

*No.*

*In daylight. With the camera — there's still half a roll.*

*No.*

Kenji split a log. Set the halves aside. Set another. The axe came down with the clean authority of someone who has learned the grain of a thing and works with it rather than against it.

*Someone should document it*, he said. *Properly. While there's still—*

*No, Kenji.*

The axe stopped.

They stood in the yard with the cedar above them and the mountain above that and Kenji looked at Daniel with an expression Daniel had not seen on him before, not anger, not grief, something that had been made by the combination of both under sustained pressure, something that had its own quality distinct from either.

*You don't get to decide this*, Kenji said.

*I'm not deciding it. I'm telling you it's not—*

*You told me in the dark that you'd stop making decisions about what I can and can't know. About what I can and can't do.* His voice was level. That was the thing, entirely level, which was worse than raised. *That was four days ago.*

Daniel looked at him.

*That's different*, he said. *That was about information. This is about—*

*What. Safety?* Kenji set the axe against the block. *Daniel. They already know someone is here. The drone. The perimeter walk. If they were going to come for us they would have come. They're watching, which means we have time, which means there are things I can still—* He stopped. His jaw tightened. *There are things I can still do. While there is still time to do them. And I need you to understand that I am going to do them whether you agree or not, but I would rather you agree.*

The yard was quiet. The split logs in their pile. The cedar dropping a needle. The mountain enormous and unmoved above everything.

Daniel looked at Kenji: at the man he had loved for eight years, who had walked a forestry road at three in the morning and stood in the dark and counted and come home and made tea, who had written his grandmother's name at the top of a fresh page and filled forty-three more, who had straightened the hanging scroll on the first day because it was slightly crooked and it bothered him, who had said *the moment was the whole point* without knowing he was describing everything.

*You know*, Daniel said, *for someone who spent eight years with a man who hid a survival pack in a wardrobe for four months, you have a remarkable amount of faith in my judgment.*

Kenji looked at him. Something in his face shifted, not a smile, not quite, but the shadow of one, the place where a smile would be if there were room for it.

*I've always had poor judgment*, he said. *Ask my mother.*

*Your mother would agree with me on this one.*

*My mother*, Kenji said, and then stopped.

The word landed. They both felt it land. Daniel did not look away from it.

*I know*, Daniel said. Quietly. *I know.*

*The notebook*, Kenji said, after a moment. *If we have to leave quickly—*

*One hour*, Daniel said. *Dawn. I come with you.*

*You don't have to—*

*I come with you*, Daniel said. *That's the condition. One hour, we go together, we document what's left, we come back before the valley wakes up. That's what I'm agreeing to.*

Kenji looked at him for a moment.

*Alright*, he said.

*Alright.*

Neither of them moved. The afternoon light on the yard, on the wood, on the axe handle worn smooth by his grandmother's hands and then his own.

*I'm not trying to stop you existing*, Daniel said. *I'm trying to keep you alive long enough to exist.*

*I know*, Kenji said. *I know you are.* He picked up the axe. *They are not the same thing. That's all I'm saying.*

He split the next log.

Daniel stacked it.

They worked until the light went, and neither of them spoke again, and the silence this time was different from before, not empty, not full of what wasn't being said, but the silence of two people who have said the necessary thing and are now standing in the truth of it together, which is its own kind of closeness, the kind that doesn't require touch or words, only the shared knowledge of where you both are.

## CHAPTER TWELVE

The flowers at the shrine had been there on Tuesday and were gone by Friday.

Mei noticed on her morning walk, which had become necessary in the way that things become necessary in late pregnancy, not optional, not exercise exactly, but a daily renegotiation with her own body, a conversation that required movement to continue. She walked the road each morning for thirty minutes, to the bend and back, sometimes further if the air was good and her back permitted.

Wei was forty kilometres east, on the forestry road that led to the eastern clearing, measuring ground his instruments told him one thing about and his report would tell a different thing about. He had been there twice now. He had not told Mei.

He drove home through the new signs on the old roads and did not turn on the radio and arrived before she did and made dinner and did not say anything about the eastern clearing, which was also a choice, and which he understood to be a choice, and which he made anyway because the alternative was a door he could not open and continue to be the person his child needed him to be.

He set the table. He waited.

---

She had been watching the shrine since the first week. Not deliberately. In the way you watch something that catches your attention and doesn't release it: the cup, the small roof, the flowers that someone had placed and someone was placing again, regularly, with a consistency that spoke of habit or devotion or grief, some practice that had not stopped simply because everything around it had.

On Friday the cup was empty. The flowers gone. The cup itself slightly displaced, as though it had been moved and replaced not quite in the same position.

She stopped. Looked at it for a moment. Looked up the road, both directions, the trees and the mountain and nobody.

She took her water bottle from her jacket pocket. She crouched, carefully, one hand on the shrine's base for balance, the baby's weight redistributing as she went down, and poured a little water into the cup. Not much. Enough to not be empty.

She straightened. Looked at the verge beside the road where the mountain grasses grew in the strip between the asphalt and the treeline. Small white flowers, the same kind she'd seen in the cup before, growing in a loose cluster near the ditch. She picked three. Crouched again. Put them in the cup.

She stood and looked at what she'd done.

She didn't know the right way to do this. She didn't know if there was a right way, or whose way it was, or what the gesture was supposed to mean. She had no tradition for it. In Shanghai she had been neither religious nor irreligious, simply unequipped: the rituals of her grandparents' generation had been interrupted before they reached her, and she had grown up in the gap where they had been, aware of the absence without knowing the shape of what was absent.

She knew enough to know this wasn't hers to do.

She did it anyway.

She stood at the shrine with her hands at her sides and looked at the cup with the water and the three white flowers and the mountain behind it and thought: *I don't know your name. I don't know what this place was to you or what you asked for here or what you were grateful for. I don't know what happened to you.*

She thought: *I am living in your house.*

The baby moved. A slow interior shift, an elbow or a knee, the particular communication of a person announcing their presence.

Mei put her hand on her stomach.

She thought about her classroom in Shanghai. The alphabet frieze. The thirty small chairs. The word on the board in her handwriting: *erasure.* And next to it, the definition she had written with such professional confidence, such comfortable distance from the thing itself.

*The removal of all marks or traces.*

She looked at the shrine. The fresh flowers in the cup. One mark remaining because she had put it there, because she had crouched on a mountain road in someone else's country and decided that the cup should not be empty.

It wasn't enough. She knew it wasn't enough. She was not who was needed here, and what was needed could not be done by anyone now.

She stayed another moment anyway.

Then she walked back to the house, one hand on her back, the mountain enormous above her, the road new under her feet and the ground beneath it old, and let herself in through the door that was hers now, that had been someone else's, that no longer had a name above it that she could read.

---

Wei was making breakfast. He looked up when she came in, took in the expression on her face with the attentiveness of a man who has been watching his wife for months and knows when something has shifted.

*Okay?* he said.

*Fine*, she said. *The air is good today.*

He nodded. Turned back to the stove.

She washed her hands at the kitchen sink and looked out the window at the cottage next door. The chimney cold as always in the morning: they only lit it at midday, she had noticed, which was unusual, which she had noticed and set aside with the growing collection of things she had noticed and set aside and was running out of room for.

*Wei*, she said.

*Mm.*

*Do you know what the shrines are. On the roads.*

A pause. The particular pause.

*Religious structures*, he said. *Old ones. They were here before.*

*Yes*, Mei said. *Before.*

She dried her hands. She sat down at the table. She looked at the breakfast Wei had made with his steady practical care and thought about the word *before* and everything it contained: the whole vanished world of it, the four hundred and twelve people and the forestry road and Harada-san's sign and the irori and the fox called Gin and the painted mountain and the real one and the old woman who had grown mikan where nobody thought you could and the phone that rang seven times and stopped.

She did not know any of these things.

She was beginning to understand the shape of what she didn't know.

*It smells good*, she said. *Thank you.*

Wei smiled. Set the bowl in front of her. Put his hand briefly on her shoulder, the habitual tenderness of a man who loves his wife and is doing the best he can with what he has been given and what he has not been told.

She picked up her chopsticks.

Outside the window the mountain sat where it had always sat.

She ate her breakfast.

## CHAPTER THIRTEEN

They met at the gate by accident, which was the only way they met.

Mei was returning from her walk. Daniel was splitting wood; they needed more than he'd anticipated, the mountain nights colder than expected, and the physical work was one of the few things that quieted the part of his brain that ran its calculations continuously, that counted days and rations and drone intervals and the dwindling list of Japanese words remaining on the surfaces of the visible world.

He heard her on the road and straightened. She raised a hand. He raised the axe, which was not quite the same gesture, and she smiled at that.

*You are good at that*, she said, at the gate.

*I'm really not*, Daniel said. *Kenji is good at it. I just do it for longer.*

She leaned on the gate in the way of someone whose back requires leaning. She had perhaps three weeks left, he thought. Maybe two. He didn't know enough about pregnancy to be precise and had not allowed himself to think about what her having the baby here would mean, for her, for Wei, for the infrastructure of care that was or wasn't available in the new administrative zone.

*He is inside?* she said.

*Drawing*, Daniel said. *He draws the same rooms every day. Different solutions.*

*He is an architect.*

*Was.* He heard the tense arrive and couldn't retrieve it.

Mei looked at him. Her face did the thing it did: that unhurried, patient attention, the face of someone who has spent years waiting for children to find their words.

*Was*, she said. Not a question.

Daniel set the axe down. He looked at the woodpile. He had been not-telling her things for three weeks, deploying the same careful omissions he'd used with Kenji for months before Golden Week, and he was finding that it cost more each time, that the ledger was accumulating, that some debts compound.

*Can I ask you something*, Mei said.

*Yes.*

*The shrine. On the road. Someone was tending it — before I arrived. Fresh flowers, regularly.* She paused. *Was it you.*

*No*, Daniel said.

*Kenji.*

He said nothing.

She nodded. The nod of someone receiving confirmation rather than information. *He knew this place*, she said. *Before.*

*His grandmother's cottage*, Daniel said. *He spent every summer here as a child.*

The word landed between them. *Child.* The whole dimension of it, not a transaction, not a resettlement allocation, but a childhood. Summers. A grandmother. A fox called Gin. The specific irreplaceable texture of a life lived in a place over time, accreted like lichen, like the patina on the shrine's wood, like the impression in the gatepost that had been partially sanded away.

Mei was quiet for a moment.

*The village*, she said. *The buildings are old.*

*Yes.*

*Very old. The wood, the proportions. The beams.* She looked at the mountain. *In Shanghai we demolished old buildings all the time. I watched my grandmother's street disappear when I was nine. She cried. My mother told her it was progress.* She paused. *I believed my mother. I think I was wrong to.*

Daniel looked at her. She was still looking at the mountain, her hand on her stomach, her face holding something it was no longer trying to conceal from itself.

*Mei*, he said.

*You don't have to tell me*, she said, quickly. Firmly. *I understand that you — I understand there are reasons.* She turned to look at him. *I want you to understand—* She stopped. The sentence was correct in her head and arriving wrong in her mouth, the way English sometimes did, the gap between knowing a language and living in it. *Wei and I — we did not know. What this place was. What had been done here. I want you to understand this about us.*

The axe was on the ground between them. The woodpile. The cottage behind him, the window with Kenji's shape moving behind it, the steady presence of him working through the same drawings with the same small precise hand, the same man who had walked a forestry road at three in the morning and stood in the dark and counted and come home and made tea.

*The house you're in*, Daniel said. *There was a family there. I don't know their name. The genkan — the entrance step — the wood is worn on the right side, which means someone habitually stepped from the right. For years. Long enough to wear the wood.* He looked at the gate. *That's all I know about them. The shape of how they came home.*

Mei listened to this with absolute stillness.

*I'm going to find out*, she said quietly. Not to him. To something she had decided. *Whatever there is to find out, I'm going to find it.*

*Be careful*, Daniel said. *Please. How you look. Who sees you looking.*

She met his eyes. Held them.

*You also*, she said.

He stood at the gate a moment longer than necessary.

He thought about what his government had said. The statements. The measured concern. *Deeply troubled.* *Monitoring the situation closely.* The Foreign Secretary photographed outside a building in London, looking serious. He had read them on his phone in the mountain dark, in the weeks after, whenever the apps had anything at all. He had not been able to think about them directly. He thought about them now, briefly: the gap between the language of concern and the fact of the cleared cemetery, the filed stones, the carrier wave broadcasting nothing. And then he put it away.

He went back to where Kenji was waiting at the cedar.

---

He went inside. Kenji was at the low table, the notebook open, a new page.

He had drawn the irori. The open hearth, the iron kettle hook, the precise dimensions of the fire pit sunk into the floor. Below the drawing he had written, in Japanese, something small.

Daniel sat down beside him and looked at the drawing.

*What does it say*, he said.

Kenji looked at what he'd written. *It's from a poem*, he said. *Bashō. Roughly—* He considered. *The old pond. A frog jumps in. The sound of water.*

Daniel looked at the drawing of the irori and the words beneath it.

*He wrote it in 1686*, Kenji said. *Three hundred and forty years ago. Schoolchildren memorise it. Every schoolchild in—* He stopped.

He picked up his pencil and very carefully, very precisely, wrote it again. The same seventeen syllables. He wrote them slowly, the brush-derived characters that Japanese had borrowed from Chinese centuries ago and made entirely its own, the shapes that contained the history of every hand that had written them across three centuries, the frog, the pond, the sound.

He looked at what he'd written.

*I used to find it boring*, he said. *When I was young. Too simple, the teacher said — but it's deceptive. The more you know, the more—* He set the pencil down. *It contains everything. The whole of it. One moment, a frog, the sound of water, and somehow — the entirety of being alive and briefly here.*

Daniel looked at the seventeen syllables on the page of the notebook.

*We should copy it*, he said. *Everything you know. Everything you can write down.*

Kenji looked at him.

*Bashō*, Daniel said. *And everything else. Everything you remember. Your grandmother's recipes. The name above the hardware shop. The matsutake road. Everything.*

He said it with a steadiness he wasn't sure he had, offering it to Kenji the way you offer the only thing you have.

Kenji looked at the notebook for a long time.

Then he turned to a fresh page.

He wrote, at the top, in Japanese: a name. His grandmother's name. Her full name, both characters, the ones that meant, he told Daniel quietly, *silver* and *thread* (Ginto) and underneath it her birth year, and the name of the village, and the word for cottage, and then he paused with the pencil over the paper and began, slowly, to remember everything he could.

Daniel sat beside him and did not speak and let the afternoon go.

## CHAPTER FOURTEEN

He stood at the gate and understood, clearly and completely, why he had stopped where he stopped.

Not fear that she would report them. He didn't believe that, had not believed it for weeks. Not protection of Kenji, who had already decided what could and couldn't be known about them.

It was simpler and less forgivable than either. If he told her the full weight of it: the clearing, the machine, the rows, she would have to carry it. She was eight months pregnant and alone in a house that had belonged to someone else and she was already asking questions that had no answers that didn't cost something. He had made the same calculation he'd made in February, in the apartment, with Kenji asleep in the next room. He had decided again, without deciding, what someone else was ready to bear.

He didn't know if this was kindness or cowardice. He suspected it was both, which was the same as saying it was neither.

He would tell her. Not today. When she was ready, or when he accepted that waiting for ready was its own decision, and made the other one.

---

The notebook had forty-three pages by the end of the first week.

Kenji wrote in the mornings, at the low table, with the small precise hand he used for architectural drawings, as though the act required the same discipline, the same fidelity to what was actually there rather than what you wished were there.

He wrote recipes first. His grandmother's dashi: the exact ratio of kombu to katsuobushi, the temperature at which the kombu should be removed, the note she had added in her own practice which was never written anywhere: *you will know by the smell, not the clock.* Her miso soup. The way she cut the tofu, not cubed but torn, she said torn tofu held the broth better.

He wrote place names. Every road and path and landmark within five kilometres that he knew by name: the old names, not the administrative designations that had already been replaced. The ridge the village children called *the sleeping giant.* The stream his grandmother called *the cold one.* The pine grove where the matsutake grew, which had no official name because it didn't need one.

He wrote Bashō. He wrote Ryōkan. He wrote mono no aware: the bittersweet knowledge of passing, the pathos of things, the word that had no equivalent in English and that Daniel had been trying to understand for eight years.

He wrote his parents' address in Osaka.

He wrote it in small characters at the bottom of a page and said nothing about it and Daniel did not ask.

*I photographed what was left. The ones it hadn't reached. I used both rolls.*

Daniel reached across the table. Kenji looked at his hand for a moment, then turned his own over, and they sat like that while the mountain light moved across the room and reached the far wall and began to go.

Kenji did not move. Daniel did not move. The camera sat on the table between them with its two spent rolls: the last record of names that would otherwise have no record, that existed now only in a film canister and in the specific memory of the man across the table whose hands had not steadied since he set them down.

He went to the kitchen.

Daniel sat for another moment at the table. Then he got up and followed.

That evening Kenji cooked his grandmother's miso soup, from memory, with the dashi she had taught him, kombu and katsuobushi at the temperature you know by the smell not the clock. He tore the tofu rather than cubing it. He found, in the back of a cupboard, a small ceramic dish his grandmother had used for pickles, not the wedding pot but a secondary one, cracked along one side and repaired long ago with gold lacquer in the kintsugi manner: the crack filled and made visible rather than hidden, the repair itself become the beauty of the thing.

He set it on the table though it held nothing.

Daniel looked at it.

*Kintsugi*, he said.

*Yes.* Kenji looked at the gold seam in the ceramic, the crack that had been honoured rather than concealed. *She had three things repaired this way. She said that a thing that had been broken and mended was more interesting than a thing that had never broken.* He was quiet for a moment. *She said it was also more honest.*

They ate in the last of the daylight, without the lamp, which they did not use in the evenings now. The soup was exactly right.

After, Kenji opened the notebook to a fresh page.

He drew the kintsugi dish from memory: the crack, the repair, the gold seam running across the ceramic like a map of where it had been broken and held. He drew it with full attention, slowly, as though the act of drawing were itself a form of the practice: the crack honoured, made visible, the damage not hidden but incorporated into the thing's beauty.

Underneath the drawing he wrote, in Japanese, four words.

*What does it say*, Daniel said.

Kenji looked at what he'd written.

*The break is the truth*, he said.

He closed the notebook. He set it in the centre of the table. Outside the window the mountain was becoming dark and above it the first stars were arriving, the ones that had been there before the valley, before the village, before the stones in the cemetery, before any human hand had ever lifted anything and called it beautiful.

## CHAPTER FIFTEEN

Wei's work was the kind that required precision and rewarded it.

He had always been good at this: the survey, the measurement, the reconciliation of what existed on the ground with what existed on paper, the patient work of making reality legible to the systems that needed to act on it. His supervisor in Shanghai had called him meticulous, which Wei had taken as the compliment it was intended to be.

The eastern sector was eight kilometres from the village, accessed by a forestry road that his maps described as serviceable. It was serviceable. Just.

The anomaly appeared on the third day.

He was reconciling the ground survey with the development map, a standard process, checking that the topography matched the plans, flagging discrepancies for the engineering team. The map showed the eastern clearing as undeveloped land, category three: *natural ground, no prior use, cleared for development.*

He walked the clearing with his equipment.

The ground did not behave like undeveloped land.

He was not a geologist. He had no particular expertise in soil analysis. But he had spent fifteen years surveying sites across China and he knew what ground felt like under his equipment when it had been worked. The resistance readings. The compaction patterns. The way the surface grass, short and uniform, had the regularity of something seeded rather than something grown.

He walked the full length of the clearing and took his readings and wrote them in his notebook and stood for a moment at the far end looking back along the rows.

The site was large. The readings were consistent: systematic compaction in parallel rows, deep and regular, the signature of mechanised earthwork carried out with precision.

He wrote: *prior earthwork evident, estimated six to twelve months prior, category three classification may require review.*

He looked at this sentence for a moment. Then he underlined *may require review.*

He took his photographs. He packed his equipment. He drove back to the sector office and submitted his survey report to his supervisor, Director Fang, who was from Beijing and had the manner of someone who had been sent somewhere he considered beneath him and was managing this with professionalism.

Director Fang read the report. Wei watched him read it.

*The classification is correct*, Director Fang said.

*The compaction readings suggest prior—*

*Natural compaction*, Director Fang said. *Mountain terrain. The geology here is dense. It reads differently from coastal sites you may be more familiar with.*

Wei nodded. He had worked mountain sites. He did not say this.

*The category three classification is correct*, Director Fang said again. He said it with the finality of a man who is not explaining but closing. *Update your maps accordingly and proceed to the northern sector.*

*Of course*, Wei said.

He collected his report. He went back to his vehicle. He sat in the vehicle for a moment and looked at the report and the underlined sentence and the photograph of the clearing with its uniform short grass in its parallel rows.

He took his pen. He crossed out *may require review.*

He wrote: *classification confirmed.*

He put the report in his folder.

He started the vehicle.

He drove the mountain road back toward the village, and the road was new under new signs and the buildings alongside it were old under new paint, and he drove with both hands on the wheel and did not turn on the radio and arrived home and found Mei's notebook open on the kitchen table.

She had been writing names. Not people she knew. Names she had found. The carved marker on the gate. The characters on the old beams above the shopfronts, the ones that had been sanded but not completely, that still held the ghost of their original inscription if you looked at the right angle in the right light. She had been reading the ghosts. Writing down what she could recover.

Wei read the list of names. He read it to the end.

He turned the notebook back to her.

He got up and made tea. He made it carefully, the way he did everything, and set it in front of her and sat back down and looked at his hands on the table.

*Don't show that to anyone*, he said.

*I know*, Mei said.

*And Mei.* He looked at her. *Whatever you're looking for. Whatever you find.* He paused. *Be careful what you do with it. We have the baby to—*

*I know*, she said. *Wei. I know.*

He nodded. He picked up his tea.

Outside the window the mountain sat where it had always sat. The cottage next door, the cold chimney, the cedar tree moving in the evening draft. A light on inside: dim, well back from the window, the careful light of people who have learned to inhabit darkness.

They sat together at the kitchen table until it was fully dark and neither of them said anything else and the tea went cold and the baby shifted and the valley went on being what it now was, which was not what it had been, which was the thing neither of them could say and both of them knew.

---

## CHAPTER SIXTEEN

Daniel saw it at ten past nine.

He was at the kitchen window when the drone appeared over the eastern ridge and did not follow the expected line.

Every drone he had tracked for three weeks had followed one of two paths: the valley sweep, low and fast, moving northeast to southwest along the floor of the valley; or the ridge pass, high and steady, crossing the mountain above them without descending. He had logged them in the back of the notebook in pencil, times and approximate bearings, the way you log weather when weather matters.

This one came over the eastern ridge and turned south.

Toward the village. Toward the road. Toward the cedar tree and the cottage and the forty metres of track between their gate and Mei's.

*Kenji*, he said.

Kenji was across the room before the word finished. He looked where Daniel was looking.

The drone was lower than the ridge-pass drones. Slower. It moved with the deliberate pace of something looking rather than passing, a different gait, a different purpose. It had a sensor array beneath the body that caught the last of the evening light as it turned.

*Cellar*, Daniel said.

They had prepared this. They had prepared it the first week, moving the entrance rug, clearing the trap door, stocking the space with water and the emergency blankets and the small hand-crank radio and two days of rations.

Kenji took the notebook. Daniel took the camera and the pack.

They went down.

---

The cellar was low and cold and smelled of earth and the ghost of root vegetables stored there over decades, a smell that was specific and old and which Kenji had said, the first time they'd opened the hatch, smelled like his grandmother's hands in autumn. It was three metres by two. Enough to sit, not to stand.

Daniel pulled the hatch closed above them.

They sat in the dark and listened.

The drone's sound came through the ground as much as through the hatch: a low transmitted frequency, felt in the sternum rather than heard by the ears. It moved overhead, or near overhead, or close enough that the distinction didn't matter. It was slow. It stayed slow. It did not pass and diminish in the expected way.

Kenji's hand found Daniel's in the dark.

They had been together for eight years. They had met at a gallery opening in Minami-Aoyama, Daniel lost, looking for a different gallery two streets over, staying because the wine was free and the work was interesting, staying longer because of the man standing in front of a woodblock print with the focused stillness of someone who had come specifically for this and was taking his time with it. They had gone for ramen afterward. They had gone for ramen most Saturdays after that.

Eight years. The painting class and the bike rides and the konbini stops and the long Sundays and the slow accumulation of a shared life. Knowing the other person's rhythms so thoroughly that you could tell by their breathing in the dark whether they were asleep, by their silence whether they were afraid, by the exact pressure of their hand what they needed you to understand without being told.

Kenji's hand said: *I am here. Whatever comes.*

Daniel's hand said: *I know. I know. I'm sorry it came to this.*

The drone was still overhead. Still slow.

Then, gradually, it moved.

They felt it go. The frequency dropping, the distance increasing, the sound thinning until it was gone and then the valley was just the valley: wind, the cedar, the house settling, the mountain doing nothing at all with magnificent indifference.

Neither of them moved.

*Wait*, Daniel said.

They waited.

---

They came up at two in the morning.

Daniel went first, lifting the hatch a centimetre and listening before opening it fully. The cottage was dark and cold, the fire long out, the kitchen window showing the track and the cedar and the mountain in the flat silver of a clouded moon.

He looked at the track.

The track was soft earth, compacted but not hard. It held marks.

A single set. Human, booted, a size that suggested male. Coming from the road, moving along the track to the gate, stopping at the gate (the marks deeper there, a pause) then continuing around the perimeter of the cottage: the east side, the back, the west side, returning to the track and back to the road.

Systematic. Unhurried. A survey, not a pursuit.

*Someone walked the perimeter*, he said.

Kenji came up through the hatch.

He stood beside Daniel at the window and looked at the marks in the track and was quiet for a long time.

*Last night*, Daniel said. *While we were down.*

*They know we're here.*

*They suspect*, Daniel said. *If they knew—*

*Yes*, Kenji said. *If they knew we wouldn't be having this conversation.*

The window of the next house was dark. Wei away again, northern sector. Mei alone, eight months pregnant, asleep in a house forty metres away on the other side of a cedar tree.

*We should go*, Kenji said. *Higher. The ridge, the back path — there's a forestry hut, two kilometres above. Remote enough.*

*If we go tonight—*

*Not tonight. The track is wet, we'd leave marks. Dawn. When the ground is firmer.*

Daniel looked at him. The architect's brain running its spatial logic, calculating load and stress and the properties of terrain. The way he had been saying things like this for weeks, with a steadiness that Daniel understood now was not the absence of grief but grief itself, operating as function, as practical necessity, as the form that love takes when love has no other outlet.

*Kenji*, Daniel said.

Kenji looked at him.

*The notebook*, Daniel said. *If we have to leave quickly. If something happens and we can't—*

*It goes in the pack.*

*And if the pack—*

*Daniel.* Kenji put a hand on his arm. *The notebook goes in the pack. The pack goes on my back. If there is a moment when the pack cannot go on my back then the notebook has done what it could and that will have to be enough.*

He said this with a finality that was not resignation, something firmer than resignation, something that had come through the forestry road at three in the morning and the cemetery machine and the carrier wave broadcasting nothing and had arrived at a place that was beyond fear because it was beyond hope and had found, in that beyond, a kind of clarity.

*We can write more tonight*, Kenji said. *There's still time. Come and sit down.*

Daniel looked at the track, the bootprints in the soft earth, the careful survey of the perimeter of the life they had made in this cottage.

He looked at Kenji.

He came and sat down.

Kenji opened the notebook to a fresh page. He picked up the pencil. He thought for a moment.

He wrote: *mono no aware.*

He looked at the words.

*How do I explain this one*, he said, not to Daniel but to himself, to the problem of translation, to the four-hundred-year-old challenge of putting into one language what another language had needed a phrase to contain.

He wrote, slowly:

*The bittersweet knowledge that all things pass. That beauty is inseparable from its own ending. That the cherry blossom is most beautiful because it falls. Not sadness alone, not joy alone, the feeling that holds both at once, that knows the falling is part of the flowering, that would not wish it otherwise.*

He looked at what he'd written.

*She talked about it*, he said. *My grandmother. Not the term — she never used the term. She just — she used to say that the mikan were sweetest in the last week before they were finished. That you had to pay attention at the right moment. That the moment was the whole point.*

He looked at the page.

*I think she meant everything*, he said. *Not just the mikan.*

Daniel sat beside him in the cold cottage in the last of the night and read the words on the page and thought about the mikan trees in the overgrown garden, the dried fruits on the unpruned branches, last year's harvest that nobody had come to pick, small and brown and finished and still, somehow, present.

He thought about the right moment. About paying attention.

*Write more*, he said. *Write everything you can think of.*

Kenji wrote until the dawn came grey through the mountain.

---

## CHAPTER SEVENTEEN

They packed in the last of the dark.

Daniel had repacked the main bag so many times that the process had become muscular, his hands finding each thing without looking: the tent, the sleeping mat, the thermals, the stove, the gas, the purifier, the rations, the first aid kit, the radio, the map. The map of the Yatsugatake range, paper, because paper doesn't need charging. He had carried this map for four months without using it and now spread it on the kitchen table one last time and traced the route with his finger while Kenji watched.

The forestry hut was marked. A small square, unlabelled on the commercial map, the kind of thing you had to know to read. Kenji had told him what it was, had been there once at fourteen with his school's hiking club, a rough shelter used by the forestry workers in autumn, two kilometres above the cottage on a path that left the main track behind the cedar and climbed northeast into the pines.

*How long*, Daniel said.

*Two hours. In good conditions.* Kenji looked at his hands. *The path will be overgrown. Maybe three.*

*You should take the notebook.*

*I said I would.*

*I know. I'm saying it again.*

Kenji took the notebook from the table and wrapped it in the waterproof bag from the emergency kit (a dry bag, orange, designed for river crossings) and put it inside his jacket, against his chest. He buttoned the jacket over it.

Daniel watched him do this.

*I want to leave something for Mei*, Daniel said.

Kenji looked at him.

*At the gate. Not — I won't knock. Just at the gate. Something she can find.*

Kenji was quiet for a moment. The kitchen in the grey pre-dawn, the cold irori, the kintsugi dish on the shelf: the cracked and mended thing, the gold seam visible in the dimness. Kenji reached up and took it from the shelf. He held it for a moment, the familiar weight of it, his grandmother's. He turned it in his hands.

He gave it to Daniel.

*This*, he said.

*Kenji—*

*She'll understand it.* He said it simply. *Or she'll learn to. Either way it should be with someone who will look at it.*

Daniel held the dish. The crack running across the ceramic, the gold repair, the honoured damage. He wrapped it in his spare thermal shirt and put it carefully in the top of the pack.

*Alright*, he said.

*Alright*, Kenji said.

---

They went through the cottage once more.

Not searching for things left behind. Everything they needed was in the pack. Something else. The acknowledgement of a space, the way you move through a place when you understand you are moving through it for the last time.

The main room. The tatami, the alcove, the painted mountain on the hanging scroll, still straight, Kenji had straightened it when they arrived because it had shifted slightly and it bothered him, and it was still straight.

The irori. The cold ash, three years before they arrived and then their ash on top of it, weeks of their ash, the physical record of their evenings.

The kitchen window. The track, the cedar, the mountain, the pale beginning of light on the eastern ridge.

He stopped at the kitchen window one last time.

The garden below: overgrown, the mikan trees unpruned, last year's fruit dried on the branches. The far corner where the trees thickened. He looked at it for a moment: the place where the hutch had been, his grandmother's careful construction, the fox she had kept because it had decided to stay. Gin. Silver. No trace of it now. The undergrowth had closed over completely, the corner returned to itself, as though the hutch had never been there, as though nothing had ever decided that this was home.

He looked at it until he had looked enough.

Then he touched the doorframe on the way out. Not a goodbye, or not only. Something older than goodbye. The gesture of a person whose family had touched this frame for generations, whose grandmother had touched it every morning going out to the garden, whose grandfather had touched it coming home from the fields, whose hands knew the wood from childhood and were saying so, one last time, to the wood.

Daniel did not look at this directly.

They went out.

---

The track was cold and the light was the colour of water.

Daniel went to Mei's gate and crouched and set the wrapped dish just inside it, against the post, where it would be found but not immediately visible from the road. He unwrapped one corner of the thermal so the gold seam of the kintsugi showed. He looked at it for a moment.

He thought about writing a note. He had nothing to write on except the map, which they needed, and the last few pages of the notebook, which was against Kenji's chest and was not his to use.

He had nothing to write with that was adequate anyway. He was aware of this.

He rewrapped the corner, then changed his mind and left it open: the gold visible, the crack visible, the repair that made the damage part of the beauty of the thing. He stood up.

The next house was dark. Curtains drawn. Inside, behind the curtain, Mei asleep in the last weeks of her pregnancy, one hand on her stomach as she always slept now, unconscious even in sleep of the person arriving.

He stood at the gate for a moment.

*I don't know your name*, he said, quietly, to the house, to whoever had lived in it before Mei, to the shape in the worn wood of the step. *I'm sorry. Someone should have come sooner. Someone should have stopped this.*

He stood at the gate and understood, clearly and completely, why he had stopped where he stopped with Mei. He would not tell her now. The cottage would be empty before he found the moment, and that was the answer: that the moment you wait for is always the moment after the one you needed.

He went back to where Kenji was waiting at the cedar.

They went up the path.

---

The path was overgrown but present, present in the way of things that have been walked for a long time, that have a memory in the ground, a slight depression, a parting of the undergrowth that persists even without maintenance. Kenji found it without hesitating, leading, his hands moving the branches aside with the automatic ease of someone whose body remembered this before his mind did.

The cottage appeared below them as they climbed, through gaps in the pines: the roof first, then the whole shape of it, smaller than it looked from the inside, the cedar beside it enormous, the track a pale line, the next house, the road beyond, the village in the distance under its new signs.

Kenji did not look back.

Daniel looked back once.

The cottage was the size of a hand now, the cedar larger beside it, the mountain behind both of them vast and grey and entirely unmoved by any of this, which was either the cruelest thing or the most steadying, depending on the moment, and this moment it was both.

He turned and followed Kenji into the pines.

The path closed behind them.

The cottage disappeared.

---

*Three days.*

Mei noticed on the third morning. She had been noticing the chimney (she always noticed the chimney, had been noticing it since the first week, the cold mornings, the midday smoke, the pattern of them) and on the third morning she understood that she had been noticing its absence for three days without letting the noticing become a thought.

She stood at the kitchen window with her tea and looked at the cottage and let it become a thought.

Cold chimney. Curtains the same as three days ago (she checked, she remembered). The cedar tree. The gate.

She set down her tea.

She went to the front door and opened it and stood on the engawa in the morning cold and looked at the cottage.

Nothing moved. No sound from it. The mountain light on the roof, on the track, on the gate.

She went inside. She put on her shoes. She went down the path and through her gate and along the track to the cottage gate and stopped.

The gate was unlocked. She could see that from here: the latch open, the gate resting not quite closed.

She put her hand on it.

She stood for a long time with her hand on the gate.

Then she saw it. The corner of something white against the post, just inside. She pushed the gate open and crouched, slowly, one hand on the post, the baby's weight shifting, and picked it up.

The thermal unwrapped in her hands. The dish inside, ceramic, old, a crack running across its face from rim to base, the crack filled with gold lacquer, gleaming in the morning light. Repaired. The damage made beautiful, made visible, made the point rather than the flaw.

She held it in both hands.

She looked at the cottage.

The curtains. The cold chimney. The locked door, she could see that too, the key stone undisturbed.

She looked at the dish in her hands for a long time.

She did not try the door.

She stood at the gate of the empty cottage and held the kintsugi dish and understood, in the way she had been slowly understanding things for weeks, fitting the shape of what she didn't know around the space where the knowledge should be, that this was what had been left for her.

Not an explanation. Not a name. Not an accusation.

A cracked thing, mended with gold. A thing that said: *this broke. This was here. Someone held it carefully for a long time.*

She straightened up. She held the dish against her chest, above the curve of her stomach. The baby moved: the slow deliberate movement of a person getting comfortable, a person almost ready.

Mei stood at the gate of the empty cottage in the mountain morning and felt the baby move and held the dish that had been left for her and did not go inside and did not call for Wei and did not look away.

She stood there for a long time.

Then she went home.

---

## CHAPTER EIGHTEEN

The baby came on a Tuesday, six days early, in the room they had painted yellow.

Wei had painted it himself over two weekends in August, carefully, with the attention he gave to everything practical: two coats, clean lines at the ceiling, the window frame done last. He had asked Mei what colour and she had said yellow without hesitating, which surprised her when she heard herself say it. She had not known she'd decided.

The birth was managed by the sector medical team, two nurses and a doctor who came from the new clinic in the village, competent and kind. Wei held Mei's hand through eight hours and did not let go and did not say anything unhelpful, which was its own form of love.

The baby was a girl.

She was placed on Mei's chest and Mei looked at her, the face creased and provisional, the eyes not yet decided, the hands already certain, gripping whatever came near, and felt something that had no name in any language she spoke, which she supposed was appropriate for something this large.

*She's perfect*, Wei said. His voice had something in it she hadn't heard before. Something opened.

*Yes*, Mei said.

They had agreed on a name. Lin: clear, bright, a good sound in the mouth, a character that meant jade, that meant precious. Wei's mother's suggestion, offered gently, accepted gratefully. Lin. A name that belonged to the family, to the future, to the life they were building in this valley.

Mei looked at her daughter.

*Lin*, she said, to the baby, to the room, to the yellow walls. *Your name is Lin.*

The baby gripped her finger.

Later, when Wei had gone to call his mother and the nurses had finished and the room was quiet, Mei lay in the yellow room with the baby on her chest and listened to the mountain.

She had written on her classroom board for six years: *language is a door.* She had believed it as simply as she believed anything, the way you believe things that have never been tested, that have only ever been confirmed.

She believed it still. She was only now understanding the full weight of it.

Doors could be locked from the outside. Whole rooms could be sealed, whole floors of a building closed off, staircases removed so that what was above became unreachable, became theoretical, became, in a generation, in two, forgotten. You could stand in a corridor where a door had been and find only wall and not know to grieve it because you had never known the room.

This was what had been done here. Not just to the signs and the shrines and the gravestones. To the language itself: the one that had named this mountain, this valley, this bend in the road. Removed from the surfaces, then from the records, then from the curriculum, then from the living memory of anyone young enough to have learned the world without it.

Lin would grow up without it.

Unless.

She looked at her daughter's face. The provisional features, the eyes still deciding. The hands certain.

She thought about the notebook in the drawer of the kitchen table. The ghost names from the beams. The characters she had half-read, copied down, tried to reconstruct: the names of families who had been here, whose marks she had found in the sanded wood and the worn thresholds and the impression in the gatepost.

One name had come back to her again and again. Not because she was certain of it, she wasn't, the characters were partially gone, she had filled the gaps as best she could, but because the sound of it had stayed with her, had arrived in her head in the early mornings when she lay awake and the valley was quiet, had seemed to ask something of her that she didn't know how to answer.

*Hana*, she said. Quietly. To the baby, or just into the air of the room.

The baby made no response. She was asleep with the sudden completeness of the newly born, her face smoothed out, her hands loosened.

*Hana*, Mei said again.

She had looked it up, weeks ago, on her phone: the translation, the character. *Flower.* A girl's name. Old, the websites said. Traditional. The kind of name that had been given in this country for a very long time.

In this country. The phrase that the websites still used, that the new administrative maps had not yet fully replaced, that existed in the gap between what had been and what was being made.

She looked at her daughter's face.

*Lin Hana*, she said. Quietly. The way you say something you are not sure you are permitted to say, but say it anyway, because the alternative is silence, and silence is also a choice.

Lin. Hana.

The jade that was also a flower. The future that carried, in its second syllable, something of what the ground beneath them held: a name from the ghost writing on a beam, reconstructed imperfectly, possibly wrong, preserved anyway, the way you preserve what you can with what you have.

She did not tell Wei. She thought about telling him. She thought about his face when he had read her notebook, the long quiet of it, the way he had said *don't show that to anyone* and then said nothing else.

She would tell him one day. When Lin was old enough to ask. When the asking became possible.

Or she would not, and Lin would carry the sound without knowing what it carried, and it would persist anyway, forward into whatever the world became: a sound from a beam, half-read, carried in a name, the way all history is carried, imperfectly, by people who don't know the full weight of what they hold.

She held her daughter against her chest and listened to the mountain.

The baby kicked once, hard, and was still.

---

Autumn came to the valley the way it always had.

The pines above the cottage line changed first, the maples below them second, the valley floor last: a progression Mei had watched from the kitchen window through September and into October, the slow fire of it, colours she had not seen in Shanghai, the particular extravagance of a mountain autumn that seemed not to care about economy.

*It's beautiful*, Wei said, one morning, standing beside her at the window with his coffee.

*Yes*, Mei said.

*I didn't expect—* He stopped. Started again. *I expected to miss Shanghai more.*

She looked at him. His face at the window, the mountain light on it, the expression of a man who is finding that a place has entered him without his permission.

*Do you miss it*, she said.

*Sometimes. The food.* A small pause. *The noise, which I didn't think I'd miss.* Another pause. *My mother.*

*We'll visit.*

*Yes.*

He drank his coffee. The valley below them was red and gold and the sky above it was very blue and the mountain sat where it had always sat, enormous and bare above the treeline, the old one, the one that had been there before everything and showed no signs of revision.

The cottage next door had been empty for two months.

Nobody had come to it. No new allocation, no administrative process that Mei could see, it simply remained, its gate unlatched, its key stone undisturbed, its chimney cold. The cedar tree had dropped its first needles on the track. Leaves from the maple at the property's edge had blown against the gate and settled there, the orange and red of them pressed against the wood like a hand.

Mei had gone to the gate twice. She had not opened it either time.

The kintsugi dish was on the shelf above the kitchen window, where she had put it the morning she found it. It caught the light in the afternoon: the gold seam, the crack, the repair that was also the truth of the thing. Wei had asked about it once, where it came from. *I found it*, she'd said, which was true as far as it went.

He had looked at it for a moment. Then he had looked at her. Then he had gone back to what he was doing.

She thought he understood. She thought they were both, now, people who understood things they did not say, who had decided, for the sake of Lin and the yellow room and the life being assembled here, to hold their understanding quietly, to carry it without putting it down, to let it be part of them without letting it be all of them.

She did not know if this was enough. She did not know what enough would look like.

She thought about Daniel at the woodpile, the axe raised in greeting. His voice saying *the shape of how they came home.* The worn wood of the genkan step.

She thought about a man and his partner going up a path into the pines and not coming back, and what had happened after that, which she did not know and had decided she was not able to know, not now, not yet, perhaps not ever.

She thought about whether *not yet* was honest or whether it was the same thing as Wei's crossed-out sentence, the same management, the same door closed against what the ground contained.

She picked up Lin from the mat where she had been lying, examining her hands with the serious attention of someone encountering a new problem, and held her against her shoulder and stood at the kitchen window in the autumn light.

*Look*, she said to Lin, who could not yet focus on distances. *Mountains.*

Lin moved her head. Gripped Mei's collar. Breathed her small warm breath against Mei's neck.

---

In the evening Mei walked to the shrine.

She had been coming every few days since she found it empty. She brought water and whatever was flowering on the verge, less now, in autumn, the choices narrower, sometimes just a few dried grasses, once a sprig of something she couldn't name, red berries on a dark stem. She had no practice for this. She had looked nothing up. She came and filled the cup and stood for a moment and went home.

Tonight she came with Lin in the carrier against her chest, the baby's face turned outward, the mountain air on her cheeks for the first time.

The shrine at the bend. The small wooden roof. The cup, empty, she had been the last one here, five days ago. She poured water. She put in the dried grasses.

She stood.

Lin looked at the mountain with the unfocused openness of a person for whom everything is equally new, for whom a shrine and a tree and a sky are all the same category of thing: present, interesting, passing.

*This was here before us*, Mei said. To Lin, or to whoever had built it, or to herself. *I don't know whose it was. I'm going to keep it anyway.*

She stood for a while longer.

The mountain did not respond. The pines moved in the evening wind. The cup held its water and its grasses.

Mei turned and walked home on the road that had been a road for a long time, in the valley that had been a valley for longer, under the mountains that had been mountains before anything with a name had stood beneath them and looked up.

Lin slept against her chest.

Mei walked.
