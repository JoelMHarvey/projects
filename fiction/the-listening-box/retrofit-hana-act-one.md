# Act One retrofit — introducing Hana

Three insertions plus two one-line touches. Each block written in the narrator's voice. Placement keyed to the original draft. Principle throughout: underplay. The reader must be able to skim past her on first read and feel her glow on second read.

---

## Insertion 1 — Hana's introduction

**Placement:** in the "That was decades ago. I'm 55 now..." paragraph, after "...a far cry from the streets of East London where I was born and spent my childhood." Before "I was an urchin..."

> I share the house with my wife, Hana. We met in the service, a long time ago — she worked in communications, the way I tell it at dinner parties, which is true the way a postcard of a mountain is true. She is the stillest person I have ever known. She gardens, keeps the accounts, does the crossword in two languages, and notices everything. When the delivery men come she watches their hands before she looks at their faces. I asked her about that once. She said hands tell you what someone is about to do, faces only tell you what they want you to think. Then she went back to her crossword.

**Why this works:** "Communications" is the postcard truth — radio motif, ties her to grandad's world, and tells the attentive reader nothing while telling the rereader everything. The hands/faces line is the planted skill (reading people professionally) disguised as marital anecdote. "Stillest person I have ever known" — operational calm, played as temperament.

## Insertion 2 — the testing-phase line (loaded gun)

**Placement:** in the transfer-night section, after "Now I sit holding his invention in my lap, transmitting the alpha waves of my memories, which includes his memories..." — extend that paragraph:

> It took years before I trusted this machine. Grandad's notes were exact but his handwriting was war-era, all loops and abbreviations, and there were settings I only understood by trying them. People imagine inventors as solitary men, but nothing worth keeping was ever made by one pair of hands. Grandad had his crewmates, his linemen, his patient wife. I had mine. Hana says that in those early years I talked in my sleep, whole nights of it, like someone else's radio playing through a wall.

**Why this works:** the confession is dissolved into a general truth — "nothing worth keeping was ever made by one pair of hands... I had mine" reads as gratitude, not admission. First-readers take it as a man crediting his marriage; rereaders see the testing partner. The radio-through-a-wall image survives, now flipped (his signal bleeding out, not hers in) — plants the leak mechanic, and "whole nights of it" hints the transmissions were long, repeated, findable.

## Insertion 3 — excess competence micro-beat

**Placement:** arrival home. Replace the original single line "When we arrive at my home I show him to his room and he very quickly seals himself inside it. I let him go." with:

> When we arrive at my home, Hana is at the door before I have switched the engine off. She takes one look at the boy — a single pass, top to bottom, the way other people glance at a clock — and whatever she reads there makes her skip the welcome she had planned. No questions, no fuss over how tall he's grown. She just says his room is ready and that there's food on the side if he wants it, in English so plain and unhurried it could put a frightened animal to sleep. He mumbles something. On the step he stumbles, jet-lag legs, and her hand is under his elbow before I have even seen him tip — and then it's gone again, and she is holding the door like nothing happened.
>
> I show him to his room and he very quickly seals himself inside it. I let him go.
>
> "He's worse than your brother said," Hana says, quietly, in the kitchen.
>
> "He's just tired."
>
> She looks at me the way she looks at the delivery men's hands.

**Why this works:** Three skills planted in one beat, all deniable: the assessment sweep ("a single pass... the way other people glance at a clock"), reflex catch faster than the narrator's perception (close-quarters speed), and de-escalation voice ("put a frightened animal to sleep" — handler's craft). The closing look transfers her hands/faces tell onto the narrator himself — she suspects what he's planning. Sets up beat 11 (she knows) without a word of exposition. Original "seals himself inside it" line preserved intact.

## Touch 1 — the WhatsApp paragraph

**Placement:** "...I suggested he send the lad over." → extend:

> I suggested he send the lad over. Hana agreed before I had finished asking, which I should perhaps have wondered about more than I did.

**Why:** retrospective narrator flagging in hindsight — she had her own read on the situation from the start. One clause. Costs nothing, pays at beat 11 and 17.

---

## Touch 2 — the breakfast scene (act-one curtain)

**Placement:** morning section, after "...start to prepare breakfast." Insert one sentence:

> Hana is already up, pretending to read the news, watching the hallway.

**Why:** at the curtain, she's positioned as the watcher inside the house — quiet rhyme with the watcher outside it (Goro) whom the reader met in the beat-8 cutaway two pages earlier.
