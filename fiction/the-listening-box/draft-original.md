# Original draft (untouched reference)

Since he passed it to me, I’ve kept it safe, locked away from dust and danger. Through every move, every country, it has always been ported safely in my hands. Someone else’s shoes carrying someone else’s steps. Never boxed up with other possessions. Each time I move I trim down the amount of junk I carry, but this box stays. Grandad’s box. Not a book, but a vessel containing everything he learned and more, perhaps something that could even change the world. As I get older I realise my responsibility, when the time comes, to pass this on. That time is fast approaching.

It doesn’t look much, an old Clarks shoe box, except it has a coating of some form of wax to keep it strong, so it looks new, the crimson lettering still preserved. I first saw it in his shed when I was about 9, it was up on a shelf, with all those neatly laid out tools, wrenches, spanners, things I never knew the names of. I used to ask what each one was called and what it did. Grandad would patiently explain, checking each time that I understood. I learned so much from him even then. He used to call me ‘Joe 90’ after the TV programme, there is a Joe 90 sticker on the side of the shoe box, protected too from aging by the wax coating.

That’s where he got the idea, that TV show with the puppet boy sitting in a spinning cage called a Rat Trap, the BIG RAT or Brain Impulse Galvanascope Record and Transfer device gave that young spy the memories and skills of a nuclear physicist or brain surgeon, whatever was needed for him to complete the mission. The show was from the late sixties, but I watched the re-runs each Saturday morning.

That was decades ago. I’m 55 now, living in my own futuristic little house outside Tokyo, a far cry from the streets of East London where I was born and spent my childhood. I was an urchin, a latch-key kid from a single-parent family. One of three, a middle child, I started fires, stole sweets from newsagents, lied to adults, associated with a bad crowd, smoked cigarettes at fourteen, weed at sixteen and I would have gone all the way to prison if not for those fateful six weeks with my Granddad in the summer of 1989. Mum sent me there when I was all messed up, she thought he’d know what to do to help me.

A couple of days ago I got a WhatsApp from my Brother, his son is struggling a bit. He’s what the Japanese call hikikomori, a shut-in. Spends all day online gaming in his nightgown, the state of his room is the subject of continuous disagreements. He’s 23, has no job, had a girlfriend but that ended, he lost his best friend to suicide and has since confessed to having panic attacks. He lives in a small town outside Oxford and has never really been anywhere. I suggested he send the lad over. Autumn in Japan is a short and wonderful season, maybe I can do for him, what was once done for me. Although I am a poor substitute for my Grandfather.

Today I picked him up from Haneda airport, I last saw him when he was about 8, so I was shocked at how much he looks just like my brother did when he was young, he has a lost look in his eyes, can’t make full sentences and the car journey back was full of uncomfortable silences as I tried to pull sentences out of him, but got single-word responses, like pulling out seeds from a pomegranate with a needle. I can tell he is from a small town, overwhelmed by the new experience, tired from a long flight and unable to process being in Japan. He’s a little grumpy like an over-tired child and will need time and sleep to recover.

When we arrive at my home I show him to his room and he very quickly seals himself inside it. I let him go.

That night he had the most fascinating dream. The same one I once had.

The dream was a story of a man who had served as a warrant officer on several ships and a submarine in the second world war. He would spend days and long nights listening for the enemy, the sonar thumping like a heartbeat. Static hissing in his headphones. The black and endless ocean separated by a thin steel wall. There would be chases and false alarms, pods of whales mistaken for German mines. After surviving a torpedo hit to his ship, they waited in the icy cold night sea to be rescued.

The dream that my troubled nephew was now having was the same one I had experienced back when I was roughly his age. The true life story of Felix Usher, a young Naval radio operator, and then later a surveyor and engineer installing UHF/VHF radio networks around the world for Telecom companies, a family man, and then a serene Grandfather tinkering away in his shed.

Now I sit holding his invention in my lap, transmitting the alpha waves of my memories, which includes his memories, passing on the skills that he passed onto me and that have helped me to lead a productive and happy life.

Along with Grandad’s fascinating life, my own memories, my knowledge of Asia, and language skills, my grandad’s ability to make things, my years spent meditating, his wisdom, my mistakes…all this is being passed on to my unsuspecting nephew while he sleeps.

In the morning I get up early as usual, meditate for a while and then start to prepare breakfast. I hear movement as I suspect the smell of the rice cooker and steamed fish pulls him out of his hiding place and into the kitchen.

He looks at me like he knows me. For a moment, I think he truly does, not as an uncle, but as someone who’s lived my memories. I expect him to quote the Matrix movie and say “I know Kung Fu”!
