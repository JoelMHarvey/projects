# The Listening Box

*Working draft — assembled manuscript v1*

---

## One

Since he passed it to me, I've kept it safe, locked away from dust and danger. Through every move, every country, it has been carried in my hands — someone else's shoes carrying someone else's steps, never boxed up with the other possessions. Each time I move I trim down the junk, but this box stays. Grandad's box. Not a book, but a vessel containing everything he learned and more. As I get older I realise my responsibility, when the time comes, to pass it on. That time is fast approaching.

It doesn't look much: an old Clarks shoe box under a coating of wax that has kept the crimson lettering bright. I first saw it in his shed when I was about nine, up on a shelf above the neatly laid out tools I used to ask him to name. He would explain each one — what it was called, what it did — checking every time that I understood. He called me 'Joe 90', after the old puppet show where a boy in a spinning cage was given the memories and skills of whoever the mission required: nuclear physicist, brain surgeon, pilot. There is a Joe 90 sticker on the side of the box, preserved too under the wax. That is where he got the idea.

That was decades ago. I'm 55 now, living in my own futuristic little house outside Tokyo, a far cry from the streets of East London where I was born and spent my childhood. I share the house with my wife, Hana. We met in the service, a long time ago — she worked in communications, the way I tell it at dinner parties, which is true the way a postcard of a mountain is true. She is the stillest person I have ever known. She gardens, keeps the accounts, does the crossword in two languages, and notices everything. When the delivery men come she watches their hands before she looks at their faces. I asked her about that once. She said hands tell you what someone is about to do, faces only tell you what they want you to think. Then she went back to her crossword.

I was an urchin, a latch-key kid from a single-parent family. One of three, a middle child. I started fires, stole sweets from newsagents, lied to adults, associated with a bad crowd, smoked cigarettes at fourteen, weed at sixteen, and I would have gone all the way to prison if not for those fateful six weeks with my Grandad in the summer of 1989, when I was eighteen and accelerating. Mum sent me there when I was all messed up; she thought he'd know what to do to help me.

A couple of days ago I got a WhatsApp from my brother. His son is struggling a bit. He's what the Japanese call hikikomori, a shut-in. Spends all day online gaming in his dressing gown; the state of his room is the subject of continuous disagreements. He's 23, has no job, had a girlfriend but that ended, he lost his best friend to suicide and has since confessed to having panic attacks. He lives in a small town outside Oxford and has never really been anywhere. I suggested he send the lad over. Hana agreed before I had finished asking, which I should perhaps have wondered about more than I did. Autumn in Japan is a short and wonderful season; maybe I can do for him what was once done for me. Although I am a poor substitute for my Grandfather.

Today I picked him up from Haneda airport. I last saw him when he was about 8, so I was shocked at how much he looks just like my brother did when he was young. He has a lost look in his eyes, can't make full sentences, and the car journey back was full of uncomfortable silences as I tried to pull sentences out of him, but got single-word responses, like pulling out seeds from a pomegranate with a needle. I can tell he is from a small town, overwhelmed by the new experience, tired from a long flight and unable to process being in Japan. He's a little grumpy, like an over-tired child, and will need time and sleep to recover.

When we arrive at my home, Hana is at the door before I have switched the engine off. She takes one look at the boy — a single pass, top to bottom, the way other people glance at a clock — and whatever she reads there makes her skip the welcome she had planned. No questions, no fuss over how tall he's grown. She just says his room is ready and that there's food on the side if he wants it, in English so plain and unhurried it could put a frightened animal to sleep. He mumbles something. On the step he stumbles, jet-lag legs, and her hand is under his elbow before I have even seen him tip — and then it's gone again, and she is holding the door like nothing happened.

I show him to his room and he very quickly seals himself inside it. I let him go.

"He's worse than your brother said," Hana says, quietly, in the kitchen.

"He's just tired."

She looks at me the way she looks at the delivery men's hands.

## Two

Twenty-six hours door to door and the last hour is the worst, because Uncle Joe keeps trying.

He does it like a man fishing without bait. How was the flight. Long. Are you hungry. Not really. Your dad says you're into your games — what is it you play? And what is Daniel supposed to say to that. He plays four things and each of them would take an hour to explain to a man who says *into your games* like games were stamps, so he says, "Different stuff," and watches Tokyo assemble itself out the window, kilometre after kilometre of it, like a level loading faster than he can render.

He knows he's being hard work. He can hear himself doing it. There's a version of Daniel about a metre behind his own head who watches him give these one-word answers and says, mate, the man flew you here, he's trying — and Daniel agrees with that version completely and cannot do a single thing about it. That's the part nobody gets. It's not that he won't. The sentences just don't come out. They form up somewhere behind his teeth and then disband, like they've checked the odds and thought better of it.

Ross would have loved this. That's the thought that arrives, uninvited, somewhere on the expressway — Ross doing his terrible Japanese accent off the anime subs, Ross planning the whole itinerary in a Google doc he'd never finish. The thought sits down next to Daniel and stays for the rest of the drive, the way it does. Eleven months now. People say it gets smaller. It doesn't get smaller. You just build more around it.

The house is fine. Modern, low, kind of a flex if he's honest — solar tiles, a wall that's mostly glass. The aunt is waiting at the door.

He'd forgotten there was an aunt.

She's small and round-shouldered in a beige cardigan, grey hair pinned up off her neck, and she looks at him for a second — just a second, like she's reading the price on him — and then doesn't hug him, thank god, doesn't do the *look how tall* thing, just says his room is ready and there's food on the side if he wants it. Her English is slow and flat and easy to follow, which after twenty-six hours he is grudgingly grateful for. He trips on the step — legs gone to static somewhere over Siberia — and somehow doesn't fall, catches himself on something, her arm maybe, he's too tired to reconstruct it. She's already holding the door. Inside there's a step up and a row of slippers, and she nudges a pair toward him with her foot — plain grey, his size, bought for him, he realises dimly, before they'd ever met — and he gets his trainers off without falling over, which feels like an achievement.

Tea appears. He didn't ask for tea. He has the impression that in this house tea is not something you ask for, it's something that happens to you, like weather. She sets it down by his elbow and goes back to whatever old ladies do, the crossword apparently, and that, he thinks, is the aunt fully mapped: cardigan, crossword, tea. NPC. Sweet, probably. He gives her a nod he hopes reads as polite and takes the tea upstairs, because if he has to produce one more sentence for one more adult he is going to come apart in a way that would be embarrassing for everyone.

The room is small and clean and the bed is hard and the pillow smells of nothing, of clean, and the panic he's been holding off since Heathrow finally pads in and circles, looking for somewhere to lie down. He does the thing the doctor said — five things you can see, four you can hear — and it half works, the way it half works. Rain on the glass wall. A train somewhere, long and low. His own heart, doing its overclocked thing.

He thinks: six weeks. He can hide for six weeks anywhere. He's been hiding for two years in a town with one bus.

He doesn't unpack. He gets into bed with his shoes still in sight, the way you keep the exit on screen, and the last thing he registers before sleep takes him is how quiet the house is — engineered quiet, padded, deliberate — like a room someone soundproofed for listening.

That night he has the most fascinating dream.

## Three

The dream was a story of a man who had served as a warrant officer on several ships and a submarine in the second world war. He would spend days and long nights listening for the enemy, the sonar thumping like a heartbeat. Static hissing in his headphones. The black and endless ocean separated by a thin steel wall. There would be chases and false alarms, pods of whales mistaken for German mines. After surviving a torpedo hit to his ship, they waited in the icy cold night sea to be rescued.

The dream that my troubled nephew was now having was the same one I had experienced back when I was roughly his age. The true life story of Felix Usher, a young Naval radio operator, and then later a surveyor and engineer installing UHF/VHF radio networks around the world for telecom companies, a family man, and then a serene grandfather tinkering away in his shed.

I knew what my nephew was dreaming that night, as surely as if I were watching it on a screen. How I knew — what I did, and what I used — is the part of this account I have been putting off, and I will come to it, because everything that happened afterwards happened because of it. For now I will say only this: I did not sleep that night either. I sat up until dawn in the spare-room chair, with my grandfather in my lap, and the house was not quiet, though you would have had to be a very particular kind of listener to hear it.

## Four

Two streets away, behind the shutter of a noodle shop that has not lifted in three years, a man lies on a thin mattress with his coat on, because October has started to come through the wall at night. He has eaten. That is about all that can be said for the day. Before he sleeps he does what he always does: counts what is owed, counts what is left, arrives at the usual answer, and turns to face the wall.

That night he dreams of the sea. Not this sea — a cold one, black and enormous, heard rather than seen, through headphones that smell of bakelite and someone else's hair oil. A heartbeat of sound goes out into the dark and comes back changed, and he understands — the way you understand things in dreams, completely and without surprise — that he is listening for men who are listening for him. Later there is a shed with light coming through one warped board, and a wall of tools, each in its painted outline, and a voice naming them one by one with enormous patience, in English he should not be able to follow and follows perfectly. Later still there is a woman reading a crossword in the lamplight of a kitchen he has never stood in, and the feeling in the dream is of being inside the one place the cold cannot come through the wall.

He wakes with tears in the creases of his face and lies still for a long time, holding his breath, the way you do when you are trying not to frighten something. The names of the tools are already going. He says one out loud to keep it — *molegrips* — a word in a language he has not spoken since school, in a voice that doesn't sound like his. Outside, the early train goes by, long and low, and the man who went to sleep with nothing lies in the dark grieving for a family that was never his, two streets from a house he has never seen and could walk to without one wrong turn.

## Five

In the morning I get up early as usual, meditate for a while, and then start to prepare breakfast. Hana is already up, pretending to read the news, watching the hallway. I hear movement, as I suspect the smell of the rice cooker and steamed fish pulls him out of his hiding place and into the kitchen.

He comes in barefoot, hair flattened on one side, and stands in the doorway a moment as if he has forgotten what kitchens are for. Then he sits — in the place that was Grandad's at his own table, though he cannot know that — and watches me lift the fish off the steam, and says, "Something smells unreal," which is four more words than he managed in the whole drive from Haneda.

He looks at me like he knows me. For a moment, I think he truly does — not as an uncle, but as someone who's lived my memories. I half expect him to quote the Matrix and say, "I know Kung Fu."

He eats two breakfasts. Hana refills his bowl without being asked, and he thanks her in Japanese — offhand, glancing, the words simply there — and does not notice he has done it. Hana's eyes come up over the newspaper and find mine, and what is in them is not wonder. I look away first. It has begun, and we are the only two people in the world who know what it is, and only one of us is glad.

## Six

The first weird thing is the chopsticks, and Daniel explains the chopsticks to himself before he's finished noticing them: YouTube. He'd watched videos on the plane, half asleep — that's where his hands got it. Fine.

The Japanese is harder to file. Week one, it's noise with a melody in it. By week two the melody has *words* in it, words arriving pre-translated, the way subtitles arrive — he hears the conbini lady tell another customer that the rain will start before evening, and he's halfway home before it occurs to him to be astonished. Duolingo, he decides. He'd done, what, forty days of it once, two years ago. It must have been sitting in there, composting. Things compost.

He fixes the garden gate on a Tuesday because the click of its failing hinge is driving him insane through the window, and it's only afterwards, holding the wrench — having gone into his uncle's shed, which he has never entered, and taken the correct wrench off a wall of forty tools without reading a single label — that he stands very still for a moment in the sunlight, the way you do when you've heard your own name in a crowd in a country where nobody knows it.

And the breathing. That's the one he doesn't try to explain, because he doesn't want to jinx it. The panic still comes — it came on the third night, Ross-shaped, four a.m. — but where there used to be a doorless room there is now, somehow, a door. He counts, the doctor's trick, and then something underneath the counting takes over, slow and patterned and completely confident, like hands that have done a thing ten thousand times taking the work off him, and the engine winds down in four minutes instead of forty. He lies in the dark afterwards almost laughing. *Maybe Japan is fixing me*, he thinks. He is careful not to think it too loudly.

He starts coming downstairs more. He'd be embarrassed for anyone to point this out, so nobody does, though twice he catches the aunt noticing — that price-reading flick of a glance — and once, weirdly, he could swear she looks not pleased but *worried*, which makes no sense and he doesn't pursue it. He helps his uncle clear the gutters. He walks to the station and back just to do it. He plays less. He sleeps like the dead, every night, and wakes up every morning feeling — there's no other word for it, and it's a word he'd never say out loud — *accompanied*.

Six weeks, he'd thought, hiding. It's been three, and he has started, cautiously, the way you test ice, to want things again.

## Seven

Here is what I did, the night my nephew arrived. It is time.

I waited until the house had been dark an hour. Then I took the box down from its shelf in my study, carried it to the spare room, and sat in the chair beside his bed with my grandfather's invention in my lap, transmitting the alpha waves of my memories — which include his memories — passing on the skills that he passed on to me and that have helped me to lead a productive and happy life.

It took years before I trusted this machine. Grandad's notes were exact but his handwriting was war-era, all loops and abbreviations, and there were settings I only understood by trying them. People imagine inventors as solitary men, but nothing worth keeping was ever made by one pair of hands. Grandad had his crewmates, his linemen, his patient wife. I had mine. Hana says that in those early years I talked in my sleep, whole nights of it, like someone else's radio playing through a wall.

There is one page of Grandad's notes I have read more often than any other. 1978: he tried the machine on a friend — Arthur, a lineman he had worked beside for twenty years and trusted with his life. It took beautifully, the notes say. It was gone within a month, drained out of the man like bathwater, and Arthur was left with nothing but the conviction that he had once, briefly, been somebody else. The hands kept it longest — a year on, Arthur could still splice a line a new way without knowing whose way it was, long after he had forgotten the man who dreamed it into him. *What the hands learn, the hands keep awhile,* Grandad wrote. *It is the life that will not stay.* Grandad ran the numbers a dozen ways and finally wrote in the margin, in pencil, the only sentence in all his documentation that is not engineering: *It keeps where it loves. Family only.* He never tested it on a stranger again.

Along with Grandad's fascinating life, my own memories, my knowledge of Asia and language skills, my grandad's ability to make things, my years spent meditating, his wisdom, my mistakes — all this I passed to my unsuspecting nephew while he slept.

Imagine it: not one generation but six, seven, compounding. Every grandchild starting where the grandparent finished — the wars survived, the languages, the patience, the craft. Not in libraries, where wisdom goes to be ignored, but in the blood of the morning, in your hands before breakfast. The single greatest enhancement of the human species. One old man, in a shed, with a soldering iron. Nobody will believe it, and they don't have to. It only has to be true one family at a time.

That is what I told myself, sitting in the dark beside a boy who had not been asked.

I should have been only happy, watching him come alive in the weeks that followed. I was happy. I walked around with the specific lightness of a man whose gamble is paying out, and if a part of me knew that the debt on that gamble had merely not been called in yet, I kept that part busy with other work.

Hana called it in on a Thursday night, in our room, in the dark, which is where she conducts all surgery.

"You have to tell him."

"I will. When he's stronger."

"He is getting stronger on borrowed strength and he doesn't know it's borrowed. Every day you wait, you are letting him build a self on it." She was quiet for a moment. "I knew what was in you when I took it. I read every page of you before I signed, Joe. He is walking around full of us and he thinks it is recovery."

"It *is* recovery—"

"It is, and it isn't his." The bedside clock counted a while. "When you tell him — and you are going to tell him soon, or I will — he is going to take an inventory of everything he has been proud of for a month. Be ready for what that does."

I wasn't, of course. But that comes later.

The other thing from those weeks, which I noticed the way you notice a sound under the floor of a moving car — intermittently, and with a decision each time not to worry: small wrongnesses. The garden gate unlatched twice in one week, after Daniel had fixed it. A bicycle that passed the house slowly on Sunday and again, slowly, on Wednesday. Daniel's wallet, which he lost on the station path, returned to the gatepost — not the postbox, the *gatepost*, balanced there neatly, by someone who had not rung the bell.

Hana stood looking at that wallet for a long moment before she brought it in. She said nothing to Daniel. To me she said, "Someone careful is being careless," and for the next three mornings she took her walk at a different hour by a different route, and came back the third day unsatisfied in a way I knew better than to interrupt. Whoever it was knew the neighbourhood too well to be caught walking it, and was nowhere a watcher ought to stand. She wrote the dates in the back of her crossword book and did the puzzle that evening facing the window instead of the lamp.

## Eight

The drunk is a problem three stations before he's their problem. Daniel clocks him like a hostile on a minimap — loud, swaying, working his way down the platform with that radar some men have for whoever will put up with them. Salaryman past the end of his evening, tie gone, one shoe untied. Daniel files him and goes back to his phone.

Hana is beside him with her shopping bag, reading the platform sign as if it might be revised.

What happens, happens fast and stupid, the way real things do. The drunk reaches them, says something Daniel's three weeks of kitchen Japanese can't catch but whose tone needs no subtitles, and gets a flat nothing from both of them, which is apparently the insult. He grabs the old woman's shoulder — that's how Daniel thinks it, *the old woman*, even then — and pulls.

Then Daniel is between them.

He has no memory of deciding it. There is a hand on a wrist — his hand, the drunk's wrist — and his weight is low and turned side-on, and his other forearm is a bar across the man's chest, steering him, not pushing, *steering*, the way you'd walk a wardrobe across a room, and the drunk's own stumble does the rest. The man sits down on the platform like his strings were cut. It takes maybe two seconds. It's not even interesting to look at. A woman further down doesn't lower her phone.

The drunk blinks up at him, more confused than hurt, and decides loudly that they aren't worth it. The train arrives like punctuation.

It's on the train that Daniel's hands start shaking. He looks at them, lying in his lap doing their cheap impression of someone else's hands, and the metre-behind-his-head version of him asks, very quietly: where was that filed.

Games, he tells himself. Obviously games. He's spent — he could actually total it, the number's embarrassing — thousands of hours in fighting games, frame data, spacing, reading a wind-up before it lands. Reaction time is real, that's documented, there are studies. Your hands learn things. He builds the explanation the whole ride home, beam by beam, and it holds, almost, except for the part he keeps stepping around: he didn't react fast. Reacting fast would have been flinching, shoving, the mess he'd actually expect from himself. This was *grammar*. His body had conjugated something — wrist, weight, angle — in a language he doesn't remember learning. There is no game where you learn where to stand.

"You're not hurt," Hana says. Not a question. She's looking at him over the shopping bag, and for a second — less than a second — something is open in her face that he has no name for yet, a kind of recognition with grief in it, like finding your own handwriting in a stranger's letter. Then it closes, and she's the aunt again, and she says the trains are always like this on Fridays, and at home she makes tea.

He takes it upstairs. Sits on the bed. Looks at his hands until the kettle-sound of his pulse settles.

Five things you can see, he thinks, out of habit. And then, unhelpfully, a sixth: the drunk's wrist had felt familiar. Not the situation. The *wrist*. As if his hand had been waiting, all these years, for something it already knew the size of.

Games, he tells himself again, and turns the light off, and almost believes it, and lies in the engineered quiet listening to his own settling heart the way you'd listen to a radio through a wall — not sure if the sound is coming from him, or from somewhere else in the house.

## Nine

His uncle calls him in after dinner the way teachers used to, with a kindness that means something is coming, and Daniel goes with the part of his head already doing exits — door, window, hallway — and on the desk between them, where a normal man would put bad news or money, there is a shoebox.

It's old. Clarks. The red lettering bright like it was printed last week, and the whole thing faintly waxy, like fruit. There's a sticker on the side of a boy in glasses that Daniel half recognises from somewhere deep in YouTube — one of those old shows that was the future once — and his uncle is looking at the box the way people look at things in churches.

"I need to tell you what this is," his uncle says. "And I need you to hear all of it before you decide what it means."

And he tells him.

He tells it well — that's the thing Daniel will keep coming back to, later, lying on the floor of his room at four in the morning. He tells it like a bedtime story, because to him that's what it is. The shed. The tools with their painted outlines. The war, the sonar, the puppet show with the spinning cage, the old man soldering a miracle together out of war-surplus and patience — even Arthur, the lineman it wouldn't stay inside, drained in a month, *it keeps where it loves*. The summer of 1989, a boy worse than Daniel — he actually says that, *I was worse than you, you have no idea* — and six weeks, and a night, and a dream of the sea.

And Daniel's listening, he's actually listening for once, because it's a good story — until the story arrives at three weeks ago, at a sleeping body in the guest room of this house, and keeps going.

The room does the thing rooms do in dreams, where they stay the same size but become very far away.

"You were asleep," his uncle says, gently, as if that's the *comfort*, as if that's not the entire — "It doesn't hurt. It doesn't take anything from you, it only gives. Everything you've felt coming alive in you these past weeks — that's him. And me. The best of us, son. It's the best of us."

Daniel hears himself say, from a long way off: "The chopsticks."

His uncle blinks. "What?"

"First morning. I used chopsticks like I'd — " He stops, because his voice has done something. He starts again. "I thought it was YouTube. I'd watched videos on the plane. I genuinely thought — " and then the inventory arrives all at once, unstoppable, like the long list of side effects at the end of an advert: the Japanese sliding into his ears, three weeks, impossible. The gate hinge, the *right wrench*, his hand going to it like a hand going to a light switch in your own house. The breathing — the breathing that worked, finally, after two years of breathing that didn't, and he'd been so quietly, privately proud of that, he'd thought *maybe I'm getting better*, he'd thought *maybe Japan is fixing me* —

"The platform," Daniel says. "That wasn't games."

"No," his uncle says, and has the decency to say it plainly. "I believe that was my wife."

And that — absurdly, that — is what breaks it open, because it means even the parts that don't make sense have an explanation, and none of the explanations are *him*. He stands up too fast. The room tilts and his heart starts to redline and he reaches, on reflex, for the count — five things you can see — and stops.

Because whose trick is that, now? The doctor gave him words. But the part that makes the words *work* — the part that arrived three weeks ago like an update installing overnight — that's not his. He's been doing it in another man's calm. He looks at the panic coming for him across the room like the one honest thing in his life, the only response he can be sure he authored, and he lets it hit him uncounted, unbreathed, raw, his.

His uncle is up, reaching for him, saying his name, saying it doesn't change anything, saying it's a gift, a gift, a *gift* —

"You were supposed to ask." Daniel gets it out in pieces, between the waves. "You don't — you don't get to be in here without asking. Everyone goes on about — " and what surfaces, of all things, is Ross, Ross who he never once heard knock on a door in his life and who *still asked* before he so much as borrowed a charger — "you put a *dead man in my head* and you're standing there waiting for me to say thank you."

Silence. The box on the desk between them. The boy in glasses on the sticker, smiling away at the ceiling like he's still got both his parents and a mission to fly.

"Which parts," Daniel says, finally, quietly, which is worse than the shouting. "Just tell me which parts are me. You can keep the rest. Just tell me which parts were already mine, because I can't — " and his voice goes, and he doesn't finish it, and he leaves, and the door of the guest room closes, and this time the sealing-in has a sound to it that the whole house hears.

In the kitchen, much later, Hana finds her husband still in the study with the box on his knees, holding it the way you'd hold a thing you can no longer tell is a treasure or a weapon.

"He'll come down when he's hungry," he says, not believing it.

"He's twenty-three," Hana says. "He'll come down when someone tells him the truth. All of it." And she fills the kettle, which in this house is what it looks like when she is deciding to do something.

## Ten

The knock comes mid-morning, two soft knuckles, and then nothing — no handle turning, no *are you decent*, no hovering pressure of an adult deciding to come in anyway. Just the knock, and the patience after it.

Daniel opens the door because the alternative is wondering.

Hana is standing in the hallway with a tray — of course — teapot, two cups, and, incongruously, a small tin with a picture of the Queen on it that must be twenty years old. "Biscuits from England," she says. "Your uncle hides them. He thinks I don't know where." And she waits, on her side of the doorway, to be asked in or not, and something about that — the *waiting* — makes it impossible to do anything but step back.

She sets the tray on the desk. Pours. Doesn't sit on the bed, doesn't open the curtains, doesn't tidy a single thing, all of which he registers without knowing why it matters. Then she takes the chair, holds her cup in both hands, and says, conversationally:

"He has told you what was done. He has not told you what I am. I think you should know who is in there with you."

Daniel says nothing. On the desk his tea steams next to her tin of royal biscuits like a hostage exchange.

"You looked at me," she says, "the first day, and you decided what I was. It's all right. It is what I wanted you to decide. I have spent thirty years building the woman you saw at the door." She drinks. "Before that, I was something else. Your uncle says *we met in the service* and lets people think I typed. I did not type. I will not give you the details — not because you're too young, but because they are mine, and I am careful now about who is put inside whose head without their leave."

That lands where it's aimed. Daniel sits down on the edge of the bed.

"The machine was your great-grandfather's, but the testing was ours. Years of it. He could not test it alone — you cannot listen to your own transmission; your grandfather could have told him that. So we passed ourselves to each other, your uncle and I. A little, then more. I have carried his East End and his fires and his terrible mother since before you were born, and he has carried — " a small pause, the first one, " — what I gave him. Which is how it got to you. The platform. Your hands knew the wrist because my hands knew the wrist. I am sorry. You were not asked, and you should have been, and I knew, and I let it happen. Of the three people in this house, be angriest at me. I am the only one who understood exactly what consent meant, because I am the only one who gave it."

He has been braced all morning for *it's a gift* in some new packaging. This is not that. This is someone handing him the knife handle-first.

"Doesn't it — " he starts, and has to reorganise the question. "Having him in your head. All of it, his whole — does it ever stop feeling like someone else?"

"No," she says, simply. "And here is what nobody has said to you yet, so listen, because I will say it once." She puts the cup down. "I chose to be carried. You did not get to choose. Both of those are real, and no one may talk you out of the second one — not him, not me, not gratitude. But the choosing is not finished, Daniel. It has barely started. Everything in you, however it arrived — his sea, my hands, your uncle's breath, your own games, your friend who died — all of it is just *inventory*. What you do next is the only part that was always yours. That was true before the machine, and it is true now, and it is true for every person ever born, because nobody on this earth chose what was poured into them first. We are all somebody's unasked transmission. You are simply the first one who got an itemised list."

The room is quiet. Outside, very far off, the train.

"The breathing," Daniel says, at last, to his hands. "When the panic comes. That's his."

"The technique is his," Hana says, standing, gathering the tray, leaving the biscuit tin on the desk like a diplomatic gift. "The deciding to breathe is yours. He cannot do that part from inside the box, and neither can I." At the door she pauses, and for one second he sees it — the other woman, the one under thirty years of cardigan, looking at him the way you'd look at a young soldier you were not allowed to warn about anything.

"Also," she says, "you stand wrong when you're frightened. Feet too square. We can fix that, if you ever want — " a shrug, " — or not. It would be your choice. That will be the difference."

She closes the door so quietly he has to check it's shut.

## Eleven

By October's end he has lost the sea.

He noticed it going like a tooth that has stopped aching — by absence, days late. The sonar, the headphones, the black water on the other side of the steel: gone, all of it, and what frightens him is that he can no longer say *what* is gone, only that the word "sea" has a bruise around it. The tools went weeks ago. He kept *molegrips* the longest, saying it each night like a pill, and then one morning it was only a word in a foreign language and he could not remember why he was saying it, and he sat with that for a long time on the edge of the mattress, a man bereaved of something he could no longer name.

What stays — and this is the cruelty, he thinks, this is proof that whatever is doing this has a sense of humour — is the feeling. The kitchen in lamplight. The wall the cold couldn't come through. He has lost the wife's face, the husband's name, the shed, the boy in glasses, but he has kept, perfectly, the knowledge of what it felt like to be *expected home*. It is like keeping the warmth of a meal he is no longer allowed to remember eating.

He has tried everything a reasonable man tries. He has slept in the same position, the same hour. He has walked past the house — only twice, only walking — and stood at the vending machine on the corner drinking nothing, feeling the windows of the place look at him with a familiarity he could weep over. He has been kind to them in small ways they will never trace, because some part of him is still visiting his family. He found the boy's wallet on the path by the station and carried it back to the gatepost, and the worst moment of his month was how naturally his feet took the shortcut.

A reasonable man stops there. But the dream is the only thing he owns that is not also owed, and it is draining out through a hole in him that no one can see, and he has done his arithmetic: he is fifty-three, he has nothing, owes everything, and once — *once* — the universe transmitted, into his sleeping skull, by accident, the entire interior of a life worth living. To know it exists. To know it is two streets away, in a box, on the high shelf of the room at the back, the room with the desk — and there it is again, the thing that decides him, the thing that has been deciding him for weeks: *he should not know that.* He has never entered that house. He knows where they keep it the way he knows where his own teeth are.

If the knowledge was put into him without asking, he reasons — and he is aware this is the reasoning of a man on a ledge, and he reasons it anyway — then some part of what is in that box is already his. He is not stealing. He is collecting a debt the lender doesn't know they incurred.

He goes in on a Thursday, mid-afternoon, when the wife visits the market and the husband walks the hills and the boy — the boy has not left his room in days; this he knows from the curtains, and notes, with a tenderness that should disqualify him from what he is doing, *like me, the young one is like me.*

The lock on the kitchen door is the kind he could open in the dark, and that is exactly how he opens it: eyes shut, fingers sure, with someone else's thirty years of practice and no memory of acquiring it — and the wrongness of that, the *ease*, nearly turns him around on the doorstep. Nearly.

In the doorway he steps up out of his shoes and sets them straight, toes to the door, before he has thought about doing it — a burglar in his socks, because you do not wear shoes in your own house.

Inside is the worst thing that has ever happened to him.

It is the smell. Rice and wax and some particular laundry soap, and the lamplight kitchen assembles itself around him not as a discovery but as a *return*, and the feeling he has kept when everything else drained away stands up in his chest like a dog hearing keys in the door. He knows the dent in the counter edge. He knows the crossword folded on the table, and the pen that doesn't belong to the puzzle, the good pen, *her* pen — and he stands in a stranger's kitchen with tears coming down his face, home, finally, catastrophically home, in the one house on earth he is committing a crime in.

He does not search. There is nothing to search. His feet take the stairs the shortcut way, avoiding the third step's creak before he can wonder how; his hand finds the light switch of the back room with the blind certainty of a man in his own house. The study smells of him — the husband, the brother, whatever the man is to him; the dream never said, it only said *family*. And on the high shelf, exactly where his certainty said, between a dictionary and a dead radio: the box.

Clarks. Red letters. The boy in the glasses, smiling at him like recognition.

The moment his hands take its weight, something in him goes very quiet, the way the headphones went quiet — he has lost the sea but kept this, the shape of this exact silence — when the thing you have been listening for all your life finally speaks.

He is out of the house in under a minute. He does not take anything else. He leaves the way he came, locks the door behind him out of a courtesy he does not examine, and walks home through the early dusk holding the shoebox in both arms, against his chest, the way you carry a child that has fallen asleep — and anyone passing would have seen exactly that: a tired man, no longer young, carrying something precious and asleep, hurrying a little, before it wakes.

## Twelve

I knew before I knew. The study door was ajar a hand's width and I never leave it so, and I stood in the hallway of my own house with the precise feeling I had not had in forty years — since a night in a different hemisphere that Hana could tell you about and won't — of arriving somewhere after the important thing has already happened.

The shelf looked obscene. A dictionary, a dead Hallicrafters, and a box-shaped hole in the world.

I want to record, honestly, the order in which I felt things, because the order shames me. First: the box. Second: my grandfather, as if he had died again. Third — third — my nephew, down the hall, who I had not yet thought to worry about. I am told fathers reach for their children first. I reached for the artefact. Whatever the machine gave me, it did not give me everything.

Hana came up the stairs at my silence — silence in a house is louder than any sound, she has said so for years — and read the shelf, and then read the room, and this is where I watched thirty years of cardigans come off without her moving at all.

"Door?" she said.

"Locked. All of them. I checked twice."

"Windows."

"Nothing."

She stood in the centre of the study and turned, once, slowly, the full circle, and I understood I was watching a professional walk a scene. "Nothing else taken. Your watch is on the desk. My pen is on the table downstairs — touched, by the way; it was moved and put back wrong." A pause. "Whoever it was came for one thing, knew exactly where it was, did not search, locked the door behind them out of habit or respect, and avoided the third stair." She looked at me. "Joe. Who else has been in this house?"

"No one. Ever. Not a cleaner, not a guest, not—"

And I heard it. Not a sound — a fact, arriving the way a bearing arrives out of static. No one had been in the house. Someone had been in the *memory* of the house.

I sat down, rather suddenly, in my grandfather's manner of sitting when a calculation has come out badly, and I told her the thing I had been not-knowing for three weeks, the way you can hold a letter unopened: that the machine is a transmitter. That it was always a transmitter — he was a radio man, my grandfather; he never in his life built a box that didn't sing to somewhere else; the receiving was the miracle and the broadcasting was the *given*. That alpha is a long, slow, patient wavelength and the walls of this clever house are glass and air. That on the night I poured three lives into a sleeping boy, I was, to any other sleeping head in range, simply — on.

"Range," said Hana. Just the one word; the rest of the sentence was her looking at the window, at the houses, at the dense and dreaming neighbourhood I had transmitted into like a pirate station.

"I don't know. He'd have known." I had my head in my hands. "Hana, the night Daniel came I ran it at full power for six hours. And before that — us, the testing, years of half power, whole nights. You said it yourself — like someone's radio through the wall. You *heard* it, you slept beside it. Anyone asleep within—" I couldn't finish the arithmetic out loud. "It isn't a thief. Do you see? A thief would have had to search. This man dreamt my house. He has been *inside* what I am. He knows the stairs, the shelf, your pen — he knows whatever I know, less whatever he's lost—" and there I stopped, because saying it had shown it to me from the other side: somewhere within four hundred metres of my kitchen was a stranger who had woken one morning with my whole family lodged in him like shrapnel, and had been living two streets from a warmth he could remember and never touch, and I had done that to him, with my grandfather's love in my lap, without ever once wondering where else the signal went.

"Then why only one?" said Hana — ahead of me, as usual. "A hundred sleeping people inside four hundred metres. Why is there one thief and not a queue at the door?"

"Because of Arthur." It was out of me before I had thought it. "It doesn't keep, in strangers. Whatever the neighbourhood received that night, it woke as a strange and beautiful dream and was gone by the weekend — for all of them, except whoever was close enough, and deep enough asleep, to take the full imprint. And even his is draining. Arthur's lasted a month." I heard what I was saying as I said it. "Hana. Whoever he is, he isn't just out there. He's out there *losing it*. The notes say a month. It has been three weeks."

My grandfather spent the war listening for men who were listening for him. He would have checked. The first thing — the *first* thing he would have done is checked who else could hear.

"We have to find him," I said.

"Yes," said Hana, in the tone of someone for whom that had been settled for some minutes, "and you are going to wake Daniel and tell him everything, immediately, because he is the only one of us who has been where this man is. And because," she was already moving, "if we do this without him, we are doing it to him. Again."

## Thirteen

His uncle wakes him at six with a sentence so unlikely it bypasses anger entirely — *the box has been stolen and we believe the thief received your dream* — and Daniel, who has spoken to no one in four days, finds himself at the kitchen table inside ten minutes, because there is one thing in this house that nobody else can do, and for the first time since the study he knows what it is.

They each bring their inheritance to the table. That's how he thinks of it later. Like a raid party assembling, each class with its kit.

His uncle brings the grandfather. Out of the shed — the Japanese shed, the reincarnation, tools in painted outlines — comes a thing of dials and a loop of aluminium like a wire coathanger bent by someone with strong opinions, and his uncle's hands build and tune it with a fluency that visibly isn't his, narrating without meaning to: *if it re-transmits, even at a whisper — and it will, he never built anything that didn't sing — then a loop has a null; you swing for the silence, not the sound; you take a bearing, you move, you take another. It's how you hunt a transmitter. It's how they hunted U-boats.* He stops, hearing himself, and says, quieter, "It's how he was hunted," and tunes.

Hana brings the other woman. She has a map of the neighbourhood photocopied and gridded inside an hour, and the questions she asks are not anyone's aunt's questions: who has sightlines on the house; who was within four hundred metres and *asleep* at two in the morning, that night — night workers excluded, hospitals excluded; where does a man who received that dream *go* in the daytime. He will orbit, she says, with terrible gentleness. He will not be able to help it. Pull the radius in around the places you can see our windows from.

And Daniel brings — Daniel.

Not the sea, not the wrist, not the breathing. He spreads the map and the bearings and Hana's radius on the table and his head does the thing it has done his whole useless life across ten thousand hours of minimaps and spawn patterns and economy rounds: it reads the system. Three bearings that don't quite agree — *the null drifts, signal bounces off the school's gym wall, weight the morning readings.* Hana's orbit theory plus the vending machine on the corner — *that machine sells nothing anyone stands at for ten minutes; that's a camping spot, that's someone watching lanes.* The wallet that came back to the gatepost from the station path — *that's not a clue, that's a tell; he took the shortcut, which means he pathfinds like someone who's lived here; cross-reference the shortcut against the bearings* — and the lines close on a block of two streets, then one, then on a shuttered noodle shop that his uncle has walked past a hundred times and Hana identifies in four seconds flat as having "a mattress behind it; the vent has been warm since September."

Nobody says *well done*. Hana looks at him across the closed trap of the map, and what's open in her face this time has no grief in it at all.

"That," she says, "was nobody's but yours."

They go on foot, at dusk, the three of them, and Daniel's chest starts its climb on the station path — the old engine, right on schedule, *you're walking toward a stranger who has your dead great-grandfather in his head* — and he catches himself reaching for the count and the breath, and stops, and stands in the middle of the path for two full seconds with both hands open.

Then he breathes anyway.

Not because it's his. Because it works, and he is choosing it, the way you'd choose a tool off a wall where someone had painted its outline long before you were born. *The technique is his. The deciding to breathe is yours.* He falls back into step. Nobody saw. It is, he thinks, the first thing he has done since arriving in Japan that no one will ever know about, and it belongs to him completely.

The shutter, when they reach it, is rusted to its rails. Behind it, faint, there is light, and a sound that stops all three of them in the alley like a hand on the chest.

A man's voice, low, rhythmic, repeating something. Daniel's weeks of Japanese can't parse it. His uncle's thirty years can't either, because it isn't Japanese.

It's English. Tool names. In order, along a wall, the way you'd say a rosary.

## Fourteen

It's Hana who gets them in — a side door Daniel never saw her open — and then they are standing in the smell of cold oil and mildew in what used to be a shop, and the voice stops.

The room behind the shop is the size of Daniel's bedroom in Oxford. A thin mattress against the bad wall. A coat worn as a blanket so long it has given up being a coat. And on the floor, on a folded towel — laid out the way you'd lay out a baby for changing — the box, open, its lid set aside with its sticker up, and a grey crochet of wires running from the thing inside it to a kind of headband resting on the pillow, and kneeling over all of it, frozen, one hand still raised mid-word: a man.

Daniel has been afraid of this man for weeks without knowing his face. The face is the end of the fear. He's small. He's nobody. He has cut his own hair recently and not well, and he kneels there in the lamplight looking up at the three of them — and this is the moment Daniel will never manage to describe to anyone, because the man's expression, at the sight of the family whose house he has robbed, is *joy*. Unguarded, total, ruinous joy, arriving a half-second before memory, like waking on the first morning of a holiday — and then memory arrives, and the joy collapses into horror at itself, and the man makes a sound Daniel has only ever heard himself make, alone, at four in the morning, and folds over the box like a man shielding a child from weather.

"Don't," the man says, in English, to the floor. "Please. It's already going. It's already almost gone."

Nobody moves. Daniel's uncle has stopped in the doorway as if he has walked into glass. Because the man had spoken — Daniel works it out one beat later — in *his* voice. Not an imitation. An inheritance: the East London vowels his uncle keeps in a box of their own and only takes out for his brother on WhatsApp. The man on the floor is begging in the family accent.

"Stand away from it," Hana says, very quietly, in Japanese, and the man flinches and answers in Japanese, and what he says stops her dead, one step into the room.

He calls her *Han-chan*. The name arrives before he can stop it, the way the shortcut had arrived under his feet — and Daniel watches the word hit. Hana's face does nothing at all, which is how he knows. Later he'll learn that exactly one living person uses that name, and that he hasn't said it in front of anyone in thirty years, and that it had crossed the gap between two sleeping minds folded inside another man's memories like a note passed twice.

"I'm sorry," the man says, to her, horrified at his own mouth. "I'm sorry. I know I'm not — I *know* I'm not." He says it like a man who has been saying it to himself for weeks: *I know I'm not.* "But I remember being. Do you understand? I remember being all of you. I had" — his hand makes a shape over the box, a cupping shape, water — "sixty years. A war. A wife. A shed. The boy who came in the summer, the one who was going wrong, and we fixed him, *we fixed him* — " and Daniel's uncle makes a sound in the doorway, " — and I woke up here. In this. With it leaking out of me by morning. You want to know what I took? Mole grips. A word. I held one word for a month with both hands and I lost it doing the dishes." He looks at them, one to the next to the next, and arrives at last, terribly, at the question, the only question, the one Daniel has felt coming all the way across the room:

"Why were you allowed to keep it?"

And his uncle — who has answers for things, who has spent weeks producing answers the way the vending machine produces cans — says nothing. Because Daniel can see him doing the arithmetic in plain sight, and there is no other sum: *because it was sent to me on purpose, and to you by accident. Because I was born in its path. Because of nothing I did.* The honest answer to *why you and not me* is *no reason*, and every adult in the room has just understood that at the same time, and the silence is the sound of the understanding.

It's Daniel who breaks it. He doesn't decide to. He hears himself doing it, the metre-behind-his-head observer noting, with interest, that his body has stepped forward again, between people again — except this time the threat is behind him, and he is stepping toward the man on the floor.

"What's your name?" Daniel says. "Your one. Yours."

The man stares at him.

"Mine's Daniel. I'm the boy who came in the summer." He crouches, slow, hands visible, a frightened-animal grammar he is using on purpose and only half from her. "The one going wrong. They put it in me too, while I slept. Three weeks ago, upstairs, no one asked me either." Behind him his uncle's stillness changes quality. "I woke up with a dead man's war in my chest and everyone waiting for me to be grateful. So out of every person in this room — " and his voice does what it does, and he lets it, " — I'm the one who knows. It's the worst thing that's ever been done to me and the best thing in my life and nobody asked, and there's no being okay inside of that. There's only what you do next. So. Your name."

A long time. The lamp hums. Somewhere two streets away is a warm kitchen with a crossword on the table, and every person who belongs to it is in this room.

"Goro," the man says. His own voice this time — smaller, Japanese-accented, fifty-three years old. The borrowed vowels gone. It's the first thing he has said that no one ever transmitted to him.

"Goro." Daniel nods, and stays in the crouch, and does not look around at his uncle when he says the next part, because if he looks around it becomes a request, and it is not a request. "There's a page in the old man's notes. 1978. He tried the machine on his best friend — twenty years side by side on the lines — and it drained out of him in a month, and the old man wrote one sentence about it and never tried again. *It keeps where it loves.* That's what's been happening to you. It's not damage, and it's not because you stole it. It's the rule. It only holds inside a family." He hears Hana grow very still. "So the way I see it, there's two ways this goes. We take the box home and you lose the rest of it by Christmas, and everyone is exactly what the law says they are — you're a thief, he's a victim, and what happens to you next happens in a room like this one." Daniel lets that sit for precisely one second. "Or the rule gets read the other way round."

His uncle's voice, from the doorway, careful, older than usual: "Daniel—"

"It only holds inside a family," Daniel says again, to Goro, eye to eye, the pattern flat and obvious to him now, the whole board visible the way it goes visible in the last seconds of a round. "So it's simple. It's been simple the entire time."

He stands up, knees clicking, twenty-three years old, the most certain he has been of anything in two years, and turns around to face the two people who poured their lives into him without asking, and asks them for something instead:

"We'd have to mean it, though. That's the catch. He'd know." A breath — his own; he checks, these days, and it is. "*It* would know."

## Fifteen

We did it that same week, which was the mistake, though I would have fought anyone who said so at the time.

I want to be exact about what we got wrong, because we got everything else right. The consent was real — we sat at my kitchen table, all four of us, and I explained the machine to Goro the way my grandfather would have, schematics and plain words, what it gives, what it costs, what we did not know, which was plenty. Hana translated the parts of me that did not survive my Japanese. He signed nothing because there was nothing to sign; he simply said yes, in his own voice, with the lights on, awake — everything I had not given Daniel, we gave him. Daniel watched me do it and I understood I was performing a correction in front of the person I had wronged, and that he knew it, and that he was allowing it, which is a kind of generosity I am still learning the size of.

And then I made up the spare room, and Goro slept in my house, and I sat with the box on my knees one more time and gave him what he had been dying without.

For a day it was a miracle. I have never seen a man so *replete*. He walked the garden naming things. He stood in the kitchen in the early light with tears on his face, at home, allowed, and Hana fed him, and he made her laugh twice — in my grandfather's cadence, which neither of them remarked on, out of mercy.

It began to slip on the second evening.

We were at dinner. Goro was telling Hana about the garden — he had spent the whole afternoon in it, naming things, planning aloud where Grandad would have put the cold frame — and midway through a sentence about the persimmon tree he stopped, chopsticks in the air, the way a radio loses a station. We watched him search. "The tree," he said finally. "The orange one." And then he set the chopsticks down and put both hands flat on the table and said, to nobody, in a voice gone carefully level: "It was here this morning. I knew its name in two languages this morning."

Daniel was around the table before the rest of us had understood. Goro did not weep, and that was the terrible part. He sat with his hands flat on the wood like a man on a listing deck, taking instrument readings on his own sinking — "the garden first, this time; excuse me" — and stood, and bowed, *bowed*, to us, in our kitchen, and went out into the dark. Through the window I watched him stop halfway down the path and look at the vegetable beds for a long moment, the way you would look at a photograph of people whose names are written on the back in handwriting you can no longer read.

He knew before we did; of course he did, he was the only one of us who had felt it before. I found him at the gate at midnight with his coat on over his nightclothes — that coat, the one that had given up — touching the latch, lifting it, letting it fall, lifting it, like a man checking a tooth. "It's the same," he said, not turning around. "It is going the same way." And then, with a courtesy that took the skin off me: "It is not your fault. You meant it as much as you could."

*As much as you could.*

Because we had not lied to him, exactly. We had done something I now think is more dangerous than lying: we had been *charitable*. I had welcomed the proof that I could be forgiven. Hana had welcomed a duty of care, which she performs flawlessly and has hidden inside her whole life. Even Daniel — Daniel had welcomed a fellow patient, a man in his own ward. Every one of us had opened the door to what Goro *meant*, and not one of us had yet opened it to *Goro*, fifty-three, bad haircut, who slurps his soup and apologises too much and fills a room with the static of a man braced his whole life for the next subtraction. The machine my grandfather built does not read intentions. My grandfather, who listened through a steel hull for the difference between a whale and a torpedo, built a machine that cannot be fooled about the difference between charity and family, and it had taken Goro's measure in our house — *guest* — and begun, gently, impartially, to let him go.

The cruellest engineering of his cruel situation: it was crueller than never. He had it whole for a day. He stood in the garden *being* the man with sixty years and a war and a wife, and then was made to feel it drain again, hour by hour, knowing the word for what was happening this time, and the word was not damage and not theft. The word was *verdict*.

"We'll do it again," I said, at the gate, hating myself in real time. "Stronger. Longer—"

"No," said Hana, from the dark of the porch, where she had of course been standing for some while. "We will do it slower."

I did not understand her for a moment. Goro did. I watched him understand her, latch in hand — watched the last fluent remnant of my grandfather in him meet her meaning, radioman to signals-woman, before either of them translated it for me.

"Not the machine," Goro said. "The machine is the last step. You are saying — " and there his English failed, or his nerve, and Hana finished it, in the voice she uses on frightened animals and, I realise now, has used on me at intervals for thirty years:

"We are saying: stay anyway. Without it. Lose it all, if that is what happens — lose every borrowed thing, and be at breakfast regardless. Not because of what was put in you." She came down one step. "People become family at the speed people become family, Goro-san. The box can wait. It has waited since the war. We will do it again in the spring, or we will not, and either way you are not leaving with that coat."

## Sixteen

I will write this part myself. My husband does not know all of it, the boys know none of it, and a record that flatters its keeper is not a record.

Three mornings in October, before the box was taken, I walked the neighbourhood looking for the person who had returned a wallet to our gatepost without ringing the bell. I varied the hour and the route. I have done this work in four countries, and I did it properly.

And on the second morning there was a man at the vending machine by the corner — fifties, clean enough, eyes down, holding a can he never drank from — and I looked at his hands, because hands tell you what a person is about to do, and his hands were doing nothing at all. So I walked on. I had built my search around a watcher: someone gathering, someone wanting, someone with a next move in him. I had no category for a man who already had everything he wanted of us and was only standing where he could feel it. I was not beaten by a professional. I was beaten by homesickness, which I had not thought to look for, and three days later the box was gone.

I have been wrong exactly three times in my life in ways that mattered. I will not write the first two; they are from the working years, they are mine, and the people they cost are not made more alive by my describing them. The vending machine is the third. My husband believes the theft was his fault — the broadcasting, the not asking, the years of carelessness with a machine he loved. Let him believe it; it is even mostly true. But I had the thread in my hand on a Tuesday morning and called it nothing, because I have spent thirty years enjoying being underestimated, and the price of being invisible is that you begin to believe only you can see.

It was me who said *slower*, on the porch, and I want it written down why. Not wisdom. Recognition. When I carried Joe's life into mine I had years to do it — years of his terrible mother arriving in my dreams one argument at a time, years of deciding, every morning, to stay at the table with what I now knew of him. Nobody can do that in a week, however honest the yes, however bright the lights. The machine was not refusing Goro. It was refusing the schedule.

He is in the garden as I write this, losing my vegetable beds row by row and pretending not to watch the kitchen window, where I am pretending not to watch him. Tomorrow I will plant the daikon too close to his side of the line, and he will move them, and we will have something real to be annoyed about, which is the fastest way I know to make a stranger into family.

It worked on me.

## Seventeen

What happens next is the part of the story Daniel would skip if he were telling it, because nothing happens next, for months, on purpose, and the nothing is everything. There is no way to say it that doesn't sound like the montage in a film, so:

Goro loses the garden. Then the war. Then the shed, the tools, the kitchen-in-lamplight, the whole transmitted treasury, drop by drop through November, exactly as the machine decrees — and is at breakfast regardless. That's the entire experiment, run daily: the dream drains out and the man keeps showing up, and what fills the space the memories vacate turns out to be — Daniel watches it happen and still can't name the mechanism — *the actual days*. Goro, who has never seen the sea-dream now, holds the ladder while the uncle clears the gutters, slurping tea, arguing about baseball. Goro and Hana conduct a months-long border dispute over the vegetable beds that both of them visibly live for. Goro teaches Daniel shogi, badly, his own knowledge, no one's bequest, and loses to him by December — *that pattern-brain* — and the losing makes him so proud he tells the man at the conbini, who has begun greeting them as "the family at the end house," a phrase nobody corrects. The neighbourhood, which has been quietly discussing Goro for three years and the new arrangement for three weeks, adjusts its verdict at the speed neighbourhoods do: the woman from the corner plot starts leaving her surplus leeks on the gatepost, which is how the street says so.

Daniel clocks the milestones with the room-reading Hana has been teaching him — her trade, freely given this time, lesson by lesson, his choice. The first time Goro takes the third stair without the creak — *learned*, this time, with his feet, the slow way. The first time he answers the house phone. The night Daniel has the bad kind of attack, the four a.m. kind, Ross-shaped, and it's Goro who hears him through the wall — that wall — and doesn't knock, just sits down on the floor of the hallway on the other side of it and stays, audibly, an old coat of a man, until the engine winds down. Nobody transmitted him that. He'd simply been a person to whom four a.m. was familiar.

In March, Hana stands in the kitchen doorway watching Goro and the uncle fail to assemble a cold frame from a diagram, shouting at each other in two languages with total contentment, and she says, to no one, in the flat voice she does her crosswords in:

"Now."

So they do it again in the spring — the lights on, the words plain, the yes given twice — and this time Daniel is the one who carries the box down, because his uncle's hands, for the first time in the story of that family, are not steady enough. And the morning after, Daniel comes down early to start the rice because it's his turn, and stops in the kitchen doorway.

Goro is at the table. He has been crying, in the way that has clearly been going on a while and has settled into its own weather. In front of him, laid out on a towel with the care he once gave the box, are the uncle's spare reading glasses, Hana's good pen, and one mole grip from the Japanese shed, and he is touching them one at a time, very lightly, the way a man checks his own teeth.

"It's holding," Goro says. Not looking up. Reporting it like an instrument reading, radioman to whoever is on watch. "Three days. Nothing has gone. I checked the garden first." He looks up. He has the vowels again, and his own under them, both at once now, a chord. "Daniel. It's holding."

## Eighteen

The box is empty now.

Not broken — spent. The spring transfer was long, the longest I have ever run it, and somewhere in the small hours something in my grandfather's handwound heart of it quietly let go, the way he did, with no fuss, mid-task, warm. Daniel and I took it apart on the shed bench in April to see if it could be mended, and we sat for a while over the opened chassis without touching anything, because we had both arrived at the same thought by different roads: that it had ended its service in the exact act it was soldered for, and that there are machines, as there are men, whom it would be a discourtesy to resuscitate. We put it back together, empty of function, whole of form. It sits on the shelf of my shed between the dictionary and the Hallicrafters, wax still bright, sticker still smiling, a reliquary now, which I suppose it always was.

I find I do not grieve it, which took me some months to believe. I had carried that box through every move and country of my adult life convinced it was the most important object in the world — the enhancement of our species, waiting in a shoebox for its century to catch up. What I missed is that the compounding was never in the box. Families are the inheritance of memory; they were the original instrument, patient and slow and leaky and built entirely out of love. What my grandfather built was an amplifier — faithful, like every amplifier he taught me to repair, to its input. The machine was a test, and the test was always Hana's question from the porch step: *will you be at breakfast regardless?*

One old man in his shed did change the world. I am sitting in the world he changed; it is about nine metres square and smells of rice and solder, and as of this autumn it holds five people — my brother is visiting at last, sleeping off his jet lag in the room his son once sealed himself into, and there have been long talks at the kitchen table, he and I, the WhatsApp finally face to face, and one of those talks has ended with a question I did not expect to be asked and have not yet answered, which is whether what was given to Daniel might one day, with asking — *with asking* — be given to him.

The answer will be Daniel's to make, in any case. The lineage runs through him now. We have settled that without ever once discussing it, the way the important things in this family have always been settled: he knows where everything is kept, and what each thing is called, and what it does.

Which brings me to the last thing, the thing I came out here to write down before the light goes.

It is late October. The maples have done their brief magnificent thing and the garden is full of the smell of Hana's bonfire and her ongoing border war with Goro, which began over the spring seedlings and which they would both be desolate to win. Through the shed window I can see the kitchen, lit, and my brother's silhouette at the table being fed beyond all argument.

And in here, on the bench, under the good lamp, my nephew is showing Goro the radio.

The dead Hallicrafters, down off the shelf at last — Goro's idea, or Daniel's; each blames the other. They have it opened on the bench the way we once opened the box, and Daniel is going along the chassis with one finger, naming what he touches. Capacitor. Wave-band selector. *This one's the local oscillator — feel, it's loose, that's our problem.* He learned the names from a dead man's dream and the patience from God knows whom — all of us, none of us, himself — and Goro repeats each word in his chord of a voice, and checks, and Daniel checks that he understands, and waits, and does not move on until he does.

I am fifty-six years old, and I have spent my whole life believing my grandfather left me a machine.

He left me this. The machine was just the shoebox it came in.

Somewhere in the dark beyond the garden the early train goes by, long and low, and nobody in this house is listening for anything tonight. We are all transmitting.
