# Scene draft — Beats 23–25: the confrontation

Daniel POV — he makes the call, so he holds the camera. Continuous from the end of the hunt: the alley, the shutter, the rosary of tool names. The standoff is moral, not physical; the one moment of physicality is Hana's, brief, and instantly regretted by everyone including her.

---

It's Hana who gets them in — a side door Daniel never saw her open, and then they are standing in the smell of cold oil and mildew in what used to be a shop, and the voice stops.

The room behind the shop is the size of Daniel's bedroom in Oxford. A thin mattress against the bad wall. A coat worn as a blanket so long it has given up being a coat. And on the floor, on a folded towel — laid out the way you'd lay out a baby for changing — the box, open, its lid set aside with its sticker up, and a grey crochet of wires running from the thing inside it to a kind of headband resting on the pillow, and kneeling over all of it, frozen, one hand still raised mid-word: a man.

Daniel has been afraid of this man for three weeks without knowing his face. The face is the end of the fear. He's small. He's nobody. He has cut his own hair recently and not well, and he kneels there in the lamplight looking up at the three of them — and this is the moment Daniel will never manage to describe to anyone, because the man's expression, at the sight of the family whose house he has robbed, is *joy*. Unguarded, total, ruinous joy, arriving a half-second before memory, the way it must feel to wake on the first morning of a holiday — and then memory arrives, and the joy collapses into horror at itself, and the man makes a sound Daniel has only ever heard himself make, alone, at four in the morning, and folds over the box like a man shielding a child from weather.

"Don't," the man says, in English, to the floor. "Please. It's already going. It's already almost gone."

Nobody moves. Daniel's uncle has stopped in the doorway as if he has walked into glass. Because the man had spoken — Daniel works it out one beat later — in *his* voice. Not an imitation. An inheritance: the East London vowels his uncle keeps in a box of their own and only takes out for his brother on WhatsApp. The man on the floor is begging in the family accent.

"Stand away from it," Hana says, very quietly, in Japanese, and the man flinches and answers in Japanese, and what he says stops her dead, one step into the room.

He calls her *Han-chan*. The name arrives before he can stop it, the way the shortcut had arrived under his feet — and Daniel watches the word hit. Hana's face does nothing at all, which is how he knows. Later he'll learn that exactly one living person uses that name, and that he hasn't said it in front of anyone in thirty years, and that it had crossed the gap between two sleeping minds folded inside another man's memories like a note passed twice.

"I'm sorry," the man says, to her, horrified at his own mouth. "I'm sorry. I know I'm not — I *know* I'm not." He says it like a man who has been saying it to himself for weeks: *I know I'm not.* "But I remember being. Do you understand? I remember being all of you. I had" — his hand makes a shape over the box, a cupping shape, water — "sixty years. A war. A wife. A shed. The boy who came in the summer, the one who was going wrong, and we fixed him, *we fixed him* — " and Daniel's uncle makes a sound in the doorway, " — and I woke up here. In this. With it leaking out of me by morning. You want to know what I took? Mole grips. A word. I held one word for a month with both hands and I lost it doing the dishes." He looks at them, one to the next to the next, and arrives at last, terribly, at the question, the only question, the one Daniel has felt coming all the way across the room:

"Why were you allowed to keep it?"

And his uncle — who has answers for things, who has spent three weeks producing answers the way the vending machine produces cans — says nothing. Because Daniel can see him doing the arithmetic in plain sight, and there is no other sum: *because it was sent to me on purpose, and to you by accident. Because I was born in its path. Because of nothing I did.* The honest answer to *why you and not me* is *no reason*, and every adult in the room has just understood that at the same time, and the silence is the sound of the understanding.

It's Daniel who breaks it. He doesn't decide to. He hears himself doing it, the metre-behind-his-head observer noting, with interest, that his body has stepped forward again, between people again — except this time the threat is behind him, and he is stepping toward the man on the floor.

"What's your name?" Daniel says. "Your one. Yours."

The man stares at him.

"Mine's Daniel. I'm the boy who came in the summer." He crouches, the way Hana crouched once, to the height of the gate-latch, slow, hands visible, a frightened-animal grammar he is using on purpose and only half from her. "The one going wrong. They put it in me too, while I slept. Three weeks ago, upstairs, no one asked me either." Behind him his uncle's stillness changes quality. "I woke up with a dead man's war in my chest and everyone waiting for me to be grateful. So out of every person in this room — " and his voice does what it does, and he lets it, " — I'm the one who knows. It's the worst thing that's ever been done to me and the best thing in my life and nobody asked, and there's no being okay inside of that, there's only what you do next. So. Your name."

A long time. The lamp hums. Somewhere two streets away is a warm kitchen with a crossword on the table and every person who belongs to it is in this room.

"Goro," the man says. His own voice this time — smaller, Japanese-accented, fifty-three years old. The borrowed vowels gone. It's the first thing he has said that no one ever transmitted to him.

"Goro." Daniel nods, and stays in the crouch, and does not look around at his uncle when he says the next part, because if he looks around it becomes a request, and it is not a request. "The machine doesn't keep memories in strangers. They drain out. That's what's been happening to you — it's not damage, it's the rule. It only holds inside a family." He hears Hana grow very still. "So the way I see it, there's two ways this goes. We take the box home and you lose the rest of it by Christmas, and everyone is exactly what the law says they are — you're a thief, he's a victim, and what happens to you next happens in a room like this one." Daniel lets that sit for precisely one second. "Or the rule gets read the other way round."

His uncle's voice, from the doorway, careful, older than usual: "Daniel—"

"It only holds inside a family," Daniel says again, to Goro, eye to eye, the pattern flat and obvious to him now, the whole board visible the way it goes visible in the last seconds of a round. "So it's simple. It's been simple the entire time."

He stands up, knees clicking, twenty-three years old, the most certain he has been of anything in two years, and turns around to face the two people who poured their lives into him without asking, and asks them for something instead:

"We'd have to mean it, though. That's the catch. He'd know." A breath — his own; he checks, these days, and it is. "*It* would know."

## Craft notes

- **Joy before memory** — Goro's first expression on being caught is the scene's thesis: he is not a thief surprised by owners but family surprised by homecoming. The horror-heartbreak fusion the bible orders (beat 23) lives in that half-second.
- **Begging in the family accent** — the pet-name beat doubled: he reaches the uncle through stolen vowels and Hana through *Han-chan*. Both arrive *involuntarily*, like Daniel's wrist-grab — borrowed payload firing under pressure is now an established rule paying its third time, so the reader reads it as physics, not melodrama.
- **"Why were you allowed to keep it?"** — beat 24 question verbatim-adjacent; the uncle's silence IS the answer ("no reason"), and the narration says so plainly once: luck of birth, the answer to every inheritance question. No speech allowed to soften it.
- **Daniel steps between people again** — platform-scene body, reversed: this time toward the threat, unborrowed in intent if not in grammar ("only half from her"). His self-identification — *worst thing ever done to me and best thing in my life and nobody asked* — is the midpoint and the reveal scene compressed into a credential: he alone is qualified to negotiate.
- **Asking the name** — consent in miniature: the first thing anyone gives Goro in the scene is the chance to say something untransmitted. "It's the first thing he has said that no one ever transmitted to him" — his voice arrives the way Daniel's breathing did: as authored.
- **The proposal is leverage-shaped but love-bottomed** — Daniel lays out both forks coldly (thief/victim/room-like-this-one vs. the rule read backwards) because 23-year-olds who grew up in lobbies negotiate like that; then the floor drops: "We'd have to mean it. He'd know. *It* would know." Hands the decision to the people who owe him one — the consent debt called in, payable only as welcome.
- **Not resolved in-scene.** Curtain falls on the ask, not the answer. Beat 26a (the false welcome) requires them to say yes too fast — the next scene's mistake is born from this scene's guilt, which is correct causality.
