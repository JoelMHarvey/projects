# Scene draft — Beats 20–22: discovery, the leak, the hunt

Two movements. Movement one (discovery) — uncle POV: his voice owns the guilt, and the deduction is built from his and grandad's craft. Movement two (the hunt) — Daniel POV: the toolkit sequence and the quiet reversal belong to him.

---

## Movement one — uncle POV: the empty shelf (beat 20)

I knew before I knew. The study door was ajar a hand's width and I never leave it so, and I stood in the hallway of my own house with the precise feeling I had not had in forty years — since a night in a different hemisphere that Hana could tell you about and won't — of arriving somewhere after the important thing has already happened.

The shelf looked obscene. A dictionary, a dead Hallicrafters, and a box-shaped hole in the world.

I want to record, honestly, the order in which I felt things, because the order shames me. First: the box. Second: my grandfather, as if he had died again. Third — third — my nephew, asleep down the hall, who I had not yet thought to worry about. I am told fathers reach for their children first. I reached for the artefact. Whatever the machine gave me, it did not give me everything.

Hana came up the stairs at my silence — silence in a house is louder than any sound, she has said so for years — and read the shelf, and then read the room, and this is where I watched thirty years of cardigans come off without her moving at all.

"Door?" she said.
"Locked. All of them. I checked twice."
"Windows."
"Nothing."

She stood in the centre of the study and turned, once, slowly, the full circle, and I understood I was watching a professional walk a scene. "Nothing else taken. Your watch is on the desk. My pen is on the table downstairs — touched, by the way; it was moved and put back wrong." A pause. "Whoever it was came for one thing, knew exactly where it was, did not search, locked the door behind them out of habit or respect, and avoided the third stair." She looked at me. "Joe. Who else has been in this house?"

"No one. Ever. Not a cleaner, not a guest, not—"

And I heard it. Not a sound — a fact, arriving the way a bearing arrives out of static. *No one had been in the house. Someone had been in the* memory *of the house.*

I sat down, rather suddenly, in my grandfather's manner of sitting when a calculation has come out badly, and I told her the thing I had been not-knowing for three weeks, the way you can hold a letter unopened: that the machine is a transmitter. That it was always a transmitter — he was a radio man, my grandfather; he never in his life built a box that didn't sing to somewhere else; the receiving was the miracle and the broadcasting was the *given*. That alpha is a long, slow, patient wavelength and the walls of this clever house are glass and air. That on the night I poured three lives into a sleeping boy, I was, to any other sleeping head in range, simply — on.

"Range," said Hana. Just the one word; the rest of the sentence was her looking at the window, at the houses, at the dense and dreaming neighbourhood I had transmitted into like a pirate station.

"I don't know. He'd have known." I had my head in my hands. "Hana, I did it twice. Tested at half power for years before that. Whole nights. You said it yourself — like someone's radio through the wall. You *heard* it, you slept beside it. Anyone asleep within—" I couldn't finish the arithmetic out loud. "It isn't a thief. Do you see? A thief would have had to search. This man dreamt my house. He has been *inside* what I am. He knows the stairs, the shelf, your pen — he knows whatever I know, less whatever he's lost—" and there I stopped, because saying it had shown it to me from the other side: somewhere within four hundred metres of my kitchen was a stranger who had woken one morning with my whole family lodged in him like shrapnel, and had been living two streets from a warmth he could remember and never touch, and I had done that to him, with my grandfather's love in my lap, without ever once wondering where else the signal went.

My grandfather spent the war listening for men who were listening for him. He would have checked. The first thing — the *first* thing he would have done is checked who else could hear.

"We have to find him," I said.

"Yes," said Hana, in the tone of someone for whom that had been settled for some minutes, "and you are going to wake Daniel and tell him everything, immediately, because he is the only one of us who has been where this man is. And because," she was already moving, "if we do this without him, we are doing it to him. Again."

## Movement two — Daniel POV: direction finding (beats 21–22)

His uncle wakes him at six with a sentence so unlikely it bypasses anger entirely — *the box has been stolen and we believe the thief received your dream* — and Daniel, who has spoken to no one in four days, finds himself at the kitchen table inside ten minutes, because there is one thing in this house that nobody else can do, and for the first time since the study he knows what it is.

They each bring their inheritance to the table. That's how he thinks of it later. Like a raid party assembling, each class with its kit.

His uncle brings the grandfather. Out of the shed — the Japanese shed, the reincarnation, tools in painted outlines — comes a thing of dials and a loop of aluminium like a wire coathanger bent by someone with strong opinions, and his uncle's hands build and tune it with a fluency that visibly isn't his, narrating without meaning to: *if it re-transmits, even at a whisper — and it will, he never built anything that didn't sing — then a loop has a null, you swing for the silence, not the sound, you take a bearing, you move, you take another. It's how you hunt a transmitter. It's how they hunted U-boats.* He stops, hearing himself, and says, quieter, "It's how he was hunted," and tunes.

Hana brings the other woman. She has a map of the neighbourhood photocopied and gridded inside an hour, and the questions she asks are not anyone's aunt's questions: who has sightlines on the house, who was within four hundred metres and *asleep* at two in the morning on the fourteenth — night workers excluded, hospitals excluded — where does a man who received that dream *go* in the daytime; he will orbit, she says, with terrible gentleness, he will not be able to help it; pull the radius in around the places you can see our windows from.

And Daniel brings — Daniel.

Not the sea, not the wrist, not the breathing. He spreads the map and the bearings and Hana's radius on the table and his head does the thing it has done his whole useless life across ten thousand hours of mini-maps and spawn patterns and economy rounds: it reads the system. Three bearings that don't quite agree — *the null drifts, signal bounces off the school's gym wall, weight the morning readings.* Hana's orbit theory plus the vending machine on the corner — *that machine sells nothing anyone stands at for ten minutes; that's a camping spot, that's someone watching lanes.* The wallet that came back to the gatepost from the station path — *that's not a clue, that's a tell; he took the shortcut, which means he pathfinds like someone who's lived here, cross-reference the shortcut against the bearings* — and the lines close on a block of two streets, then one, then on a shuttered noodle shop that his uncle has walked past a hundred times and Hana identifies in four seconds flat as having "a mattress behind it; the vent has been warm since September."

Nobody says *well done*. Hana looks at him across the closed trap of the map, and what's open in her face this time has no grief in it at all.

"That," she says, "was nobody's but yours."

They go on foot, at dusk, the three of them, and Daniel's chest starts its climb on the station path — the old engine, right on schedule, *you're walking toward a stranger who has your dead great-grandfather in his head* — and he catches himself reaching for the count and the breath, and stops, and stands in the middle of the path for two full seconds with both hands open.

Then he breathes anyway.

Not because it's his. Because it works, and he is choosing it, the way you'd choose a tool off a wall where someone had painted its outline long before you were born. *The technique is his. The deciding to breathe is yours.* He falls back into step. Nobody saw. It is, he thinks, the first thing he has done since arriving in Japan that no one will ever know about, and it belongs to him completely.

The shutter, when they reach it, is rusted to its rails. Behind it, faint, there is light, and a sound that stops all three of them in the alley like a hand on the chest.

A man's voice, low, rhythmic, repeating something. Daniel's three weeks of Japanese can't parse it. His uncle's thirty years can't either, because it isn't Japanese.

It's English. Tool names. In order, along a wall, the way you'd say a rosary.

## Craft notes

**Movement one**
- **Uncle's shame-order confession** ("I reached for the artefact") — keeps his flaw alive post-midpoint; he is improving, not absolved.
- **Hana walks the scene** — first on-page operational display, correctly placed AFTER beat 15/17: reader finally sees firsthand what Daniel inherited secondhand.
- **The deduction chain is the leak reveal:** no forced entry + no search + third stair = the thief has the house's memories → transmitter → "I was simply *on*." Grandad's fingerprint line lands here: "he never in his life built a box that didn't sing to somewhere else."
- **Guilt doubles per bible:** the shrapnel sentence — uncle authors Goro's suffering in his own narration before ever meeting him.
- **Hana's exit line** ("if we do this without him, we are doing it to him. Again") — consent theme now steering plot decisions.

**Movement two**
- **Raid-party framing** is Daniel's idiom doing thematic work: three inheritances, one table. Uncle = grandad's direction-finding (the U-boat trick turned against, and for, the family — "It's how he was hunted"). Hana = profiling, radius, the terrible-gentle "he will orbit."
- **Daniel's contribution is pure pattern-brain** — explicitly NOT the transferred skills (the scene says so: "Not the sea, not the wrist, not the breathing"). Bible's critical requirement paid in full, and certified by Hana: *"That was nobody's but yours."*
- **Beat 22 exactly as specced:** breathes through panic en route, chooses the technique like a tool off a painted-outline wall, no speech, nobody sees. "Belongs to him completely" — authorship reclaimed via choice, answering the midpoint.
- **Closing sound** — Goro saying the tool names like a rosary — collides the shed motif, the degradation clock, and the heartbreak-horror tone of beat 23 in one audible image. Hands off directly to the confrontation.
- The vending machine, the wallet/gatepost, the warm vent all pay from earlier scenes — nothing in the hunt is new information, only new *reading*. Fair-play detection.
